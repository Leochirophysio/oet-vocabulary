Vocabulary

10 questions · 6 from this week · 4 from previous weeks

⏱ ~10 min
Question 1 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK · exacerbation
The patient reported a sudden ___ of her lumbar symptoms following an episode of heavy lifting, with pain now radiating into the left lower limb.
A) remission
B) exacerbation
C) haematoma
D) contracture
Show Answer
Answer: B) exacerbation
An exacerbation is a sudden worsening or flare-up of a pre-existing condition. In clinical documentation it often appears as "acute exacerbation of chronic low back pain." Remission = improvement; haematoma = blood collection; contracture = permanent shortening of muscle/tendon.
Question 2 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK · spondylosis
MRI of the cervical spine confirmed significant ___, with disc height loss and posterior osteophyte formation at C5/6 and C6/7, consistent with the patient's age-related degenerative changes.
A) arthroplasty
B) myelopathy
C) spondylosis
D) stenosis
Show Answer
Answer: C) spondylosis
Spondylosis refers to age-related degenerative changes of the vertebral column, including disc degeneration and osteophyte formation. Myelopathy (B) is the resulting spinal cord dysfunction — a complication of spondylosis, not the condition itself. Stenosis (D) refers specifically to canal narrowing.
Question 3 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK · neuroplasticity
The post-stroke rehabilitation programme was designed to harness ___, encouraging the brain to reorganise and form new neural pathways through intensive task-specific training.
A) neuropathy
B) neuroplasticity
C) proprioception
D) dyskinesia
Show Answer
Answer: B) neuroplasticity
Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to reorganise its structure and function in response to experience, learning, or injury. It underpins much of neurorehabilitation. Neuropathy = nerve damage/disease; dyskinesia = impaired voluntary movement.
Question 4 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK · hemiparesis
Following the cerebrovascular accident, the patient presented with right-sided ___, demonstrating reduced grip strength and impaired lower limb function, but retained some voluntary motor control on the affected side.
A) hemiplegia
B) hemiparesis
C) apraxia
D) ataxia
Show Answer
Answer: B) hemiparesis
Hemiparesis = partial (incomplete) weakness affecting one side of the body. The key clue is "retained some voluntary motor control" — if movement were completely absent, the correct term would be hemiplegia (A). Apraxia = difficulty with motor planning; ataxia = incoordination.
Question 5 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK · vertigo
The patient described episodic ___, reporting a sensation that the room was spinning around her, consistently triggered by sudden changes in head position, particularly when rolling over in bed.
A) malaise
B) pruritus
C) lethargy
D) vertigo
Show Answer
Answer: D) vertigo
Vertigo is the false sensation of rotational movement — either the patient feels they are spinning or their surroundings are. The description of positional triggering (worse on head movement, rolling in bed) is classic for Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV), a common condition in physiotherapy practice.
Question 6 · Definition match · THIS WEEK · haematoma
Which word best describes: "A localised collection of blood that has leaked outside blood vessels, typically caused by trauma to the vessel wall, presenting clinically as a firm, discoloured swelling that may be tender to palpation."
A) effusion
B) oedema
C) haematoma
D) bursitis
Show Answer
Answer: C) haematoma
A haematoma is a collection of blood outside vessels. Compare: effusion = excess fluid in a joint cavity; oedema = excess fluid in interstitial tissue; bursitis = inflammation of a bursa. Clinically, haematomas may require aspiration or can be managed conservatively with RICE.
Question 7 · Definition match · PREVIOUS · radiculopathy
Which word best describes: "A clinical syndrome caused by compression or irritation of a spinal nerve root, resulting in pain, weakness, or altered sensation distributed along the dermatome and myotome of that nerve."
A) neuropathy
B) radiculopathy
C) myelopathy
D) stenosis
Show Answer
Answer: B) radiculopathy
Radiculopathy specifically involves nerve root (radix) pathology. Compare: neuropathy = peripheral nerve disease (more diffuse); myelopathy = spinal cord dysfunction; stenosis = narrowing (a structural cause, not the syndrome itself). Clinically: L4/L5 radiculopathy causes dermatomal leg pain, often called "sciatica."
Question 8 · Definition match · PREVIOUS · proprioception
Which word best describes: "The body's ability to sense its own position, movement, and acceleration in space without relying on visual input, mediated by specialised receptors in muscles, tendons, and joint capsules."
A) kinaesthesia
B) nociception
C) proprioception
D) allodynia
Show Answer
Answer: C) proprioception
Proprioception is the sense of body position. Note: kinaesthesia (A) is closely related — it refers specifically to the sense of movement — and some sources use the terms interchangeably, but proprioception is the broader and more clinically common term. Nociception = pain sensing; allodynia = pain from normally non-painful stimuli.
Question 9 · Collocation complete · PREVIOUS · spasticity
Complete the clinical collocation: "___ management in patients with upper motor neurone lesions typically involves a combination of stretching, positioning, and pharmacological intervention."
A) Spasticity
B) Contracture
C) Dystonia
D) Clonus
Show Answer
Answer: A) Spasticity
Spasticity is velocity-dependent increased muscle tone characteristic of upper motor neurone (UMN) lesions. "Spasticity management" is the standard clinical phrase. Contracture (B) is a complication of unmanaged spasticity (permanent shortening). Clonus (D) is a sign of spasticity but not a management collocation.
Question 10 · Collocation complete · PREVIOUS · contracture
Complete the clinical collocation: "___ prevention through regular passive range of motion exercises and appropriate positioning is a priority in the acute care of immobilised patients."
A) Subluxation
B) Contracture
C) Rigidity
D) Atrophy
Show Answer
Answer: B) Contracture
Contracture prevention is a standard clinical phrase in physiotherapy. A contracture is an abnormal, often permanent shortening of muscle or connective tissue, leading to reduced joint range of motion. Atrophy prevention (D) is also valid but uses a different collocation — "muscle atrophy prevention" or "disuse atrophy prevention."

Post-operative Physiotherapy Following Total Knee Arthroplasty: A Clinical Case Overview A 68-year-old male, Mr. Graham, was referred to the outpatient physiotherapy department six weeks following a right total knee arthroplasty. His pre-operative history included long-standing osteoarthritis with associated shoulder capsulitis, which had significantly limited his functional mobility during the months preceding surgery. Intraoperatively, the orthopaedic surgeon identified a small chondral avulsion at the posterolateral aspect of the joint, which was addressed at the time of the procedure. On initial assessment, Mr. Graham demonstrated restricted right ankle dorsiflexion, measuring only 5° in non-weight-bearing, which the physiotherapist attributed to prolonged disuse and soft tissue tightening during the post-operative period. Passive range of motion at the operated knee was 0–85°, and the patient reported moderate pain (VAS 4/10) with active flexion. Of additional clinical significance, a pre-operative MRI of the cervical spine had identified early cervical myelopathy secondary to multilevel spondylosis. In view of this finding, the treating team maintained close interdisciplinary communication to monitor for any neurological deterioration throughout the rehabilitation period. By week eight, Mr. Graham had achieved 0–110° active knee flexion and reported substantial improvement in walking tolerance, successfully completing a 200-metre walk without a gait aid. Discharge planning included a home exercise programme and an orthopaedic review at three months.
Reading Q1 · Factual
What did the orthopaedic surgeon identify during the operation on Mr. Graham's knee?
A) Significant cartilage hypertrophy requiring debridement
B) A small chondral avulsion at the posterolateral aspect of the joint
C) Complete rupture of the anterior cruciate ligament
D) Synovial inflammation requiring lavage
Show Answer
Answer: B)
The passage states: "the orthopaedic surgeon identified a small chondral avulsion at the posterolateral aspect of the joint." This is a direct factual retrieval question — the answer is stated explicitly in paragraph one.
Reading Q2 · Factual
What was Mr. Graham's active knee flexion range at the eight-week follow-up?
A) 0–85°
B) 0–95°
C) 0–105°
D) 0–110°
Show Answer
Answer: D) 0–110°
The passage clearly states "By week eight, Mr. Graham had achieved 0–110° active knee flexion." The initial range (0–85°) is a distractor from paragraph two — do not confuse the initial assessment with the week-eight outcome.
Reading Q3 · Inferential
Why did the treating team maintain close interdisciplinary communication throughout Mr. Graham's rehabilitation?
A) Because Mr. Graham's pain was not responding to physiotherapy intervention
B) Due to concerns about potential neurological deterioration related to his pre-operative cervical spine finding
C) Because his shoulder capsulitis required concurrent specialist management
D) To coordinate the patient's discharge to a residential care facility
Show Answer
Answer: B)
Paragraph three explains the rationale: "the treating team maintained close interdisciplinary communication to monitor for any neurological deterioration" — this was directly linked to the cervical myelopathy identified pre-operatively. This requires inference because you must link the reason (myelopathy) to the action (interdisciplinary monitoring).
Reading Q4 · Inferential
What is the most likely reason the physiotherapist included ankle dorsiflexion in the rehabilitation programme?
A) To address pruritus related to post-operative wound healing
B) To improve the patient's cardiovascular endurance
C) To restore ankle mobility that had been reduced by prolonged disuse and soft tissue tightening
D) To correct the chondral avulsion identified intraoperatively
Show Answer
Answer: C)
The passage states the physiotherapist "attributed [restricted dorsiflexion] to prolonged disuse and soft tissue tightening during the post-operative period." It is logical to infer that rehabilitation would target the identified impairment. Option D is incorrect — an avulsion is an orthopaedic finding addressed surgically, not via ankle mobility exercises.
Reading Q5 · Vocabulary in context
In the context of the passage, "myelopathy" most accurately refers to:
A) Degeneration of the intervertebral discs in the lumbar spine
B) Dysfunction of the spinal cord caused by compression from cervical spondylosis
C) Inflammation of the bone marrow requiring antibiotic treatment
D) A hereditary condition affecting myelin production in peripheral nerves
Show Answer
Answer: B)
In the passage, "early cervical myelopathy secondary to multilevel spondylosis" means the spinal cord (myelo-) is being affected (-pathy) as a result of spondylotic compression in the cervical spine. Myelopathy = pathology of the spinal cord. It is distinct from radiculopathy (nerve root) and neuropathy (peripheral nerve).

✅ Self-Assessment Checklist — Referral Letter

  • Formal salutation used (e.g., "Dear Physiotherapy Team," or "To the Outpatient Physiotherapy Department,")
  • Patient fully identified: name, age, gender, and date of referral stated in the opening paragraph
  • Purpose of referral clearly stated in the first or second sentence
  • Relevant past medical history included (Type 2 DM, hypertension, chondromalacia patellae)
  • Current symptoms described accurately: bilateral paraesthesia, low back pain, exacerbation with walking, and relief with sitting
  • Investigation findings summarised: MRI (stenosis, osteophyte formation, disc prolapse) and X-ray findings
  • Specific physiotherapy goals or requests mentioned (e.g., lumbar stabilisation, gait retraining)
  • Clinical vocabulary used correctly: at minimum spondylosis, stenosis, paraesthesia, osteophyte, exacerbation
  • Formal and professional register maintained throughout — no casual phrasing
  • Appropriate closing (e.g., "Yours sincerely," followed by doctor's name, position, and contact details)

Role Play 1

Post-Stroke Rehabilitation Consultation

Setting: Outpatient neurological physiotherapy clinic, Auckland City Hospital

Clinician (you): Physiotherapy student on placement

Patient: Mr. David Chen, 55-year-old male, three weeks post-ischaemic stroke. Presenting with right-sided hemiparesis affecting upper and lower limb. Has attended two prior sessions. Today's goal: discuss rehabilitation plan and address anxiety about long-term recovery.

Key task: Explain what the rehabilitation programme involves, why task-specific training is used (link to neuroplasticity), set realistic goals, and build therapeutic rapport by acknowledging the patient's emotional concerns.

Words to use naturally: hemiparesis neuroplasticity rapport

Suggested opening: "Good morning, Mr. Chen. It's good to see you again. I know these past few weeks have been quite challenging — I'd like to take some time today to talk through what your rehabilitation will look like and answer any questions you have."

"The weakness you're experiencing on your right side is called hemiparesis…" "Your brain has a remarkable ability to adapt — this is called neuroplasticity…" "The exercises we're doing are specifically designed to encourage your brain to form new pathways…" "I want to make sure you feel comfortable raising any concerns with me…" "Recovery does take time, but the progress you've already made is very encouraging…"
Show Model Answer Outline
Opening (rapport-building): Greet warmly, acknowledge the patient's experience, express genuine interest in their concerns. Do NOT launch straight into clinical information.

Explain the condition: "The weakness on your right side is called hemiparesis — it's partial muscle weakness caused by the stroke affecting the left side of your brain."

Explain the treatment rationale (neuroplasticity): "Research shows that the brain can reorganise itself — this is called neuroplasticity. By practising specific movements repeatedly, we're essentially training your brain to create new pathways to control your right side."

Goal-setting: Collaboratively identify two short-term goals (e.g., independent walking with a walking frame to the letterbox; dressing upper body independently). Frame goals positively.

Address concerns: Ask open questions: "What worries you most about your recovery?" Validate feelings, provide realistic reassurance without over-promising.

Closing: Summarise the plan, confirm the patient understands, and invite questions. "Is there anything I've said that you'd like me to go over again?"
Role Play 2

Acute Ward: Post-Surgical Mobility Assessment

Setting: Orthopaedic ward, day two post-surgery

Clinician (you): Physiotherapist

Patient: Ms. Sione Pita, 34-year-old female. Underwent emergency right knee surgery yesterday following a sports injury. A haematoma was evacuated intraoperatively. The orthopaedic team is now discussing the possibility of future knee arthroplasty depending on long-term joint health. Ms. Pita is anxious about mobilising and is requesting reassurance before attempting to stand.

Key task: Conduct a brief pre-mobilisation assessment, explain what the haematoma was and why mobilisation is important now, provide reassurance, and assist the patient to stand safely for the first time post-operatively.

Words to use naturally: haematoma arthroplasty reassurance

Suggested opening: "Hello, Ms. Pita. I'm Leo, the physiotherapist. I know you've just come out of surgery and things feel a bit overwhelming — I'm here to help you take your first steps safely, and I'll be with you the whole time."

"The surgery removed a collection of blood called a haematoma from your knee…" "I can completely understand why you might feel nervous about moving your leg…" "Getting up today is actually important — it helps prevent complications like blood clots…" "We'll go at your pace — you're in control, and I'll support you throughout…" "The team mentioned that a knee replacement — called an arthroplasty — might be discussed in future, but right now the focus is just on getting you moving safely…"
Show Model Answer Outline
Pre-mobilisation check: Ask about pain level (VAS), dizziness, nausea; check that analgesia has been given; review post-operative notes for weight-bearing status (likely partial weight-bearing with crutches).

Explain the haematoma: "During your surgery, the team found and removed a collection of blood — called a haematoma — that had built up around the joint after your injury. The wound is now closed and healing." Use plain language, avoid jargon overload.

Provide reassurance: Acknowledge anxiety as normal. Explain the benefit of early mobilisation (venous thrombosis prevention, muscle activation, oedema reduction). Emphasise patient control: "We won't push through pain."

Mobilisation: Instruct sit-to-stand sequence, place crutches, demonstrate partial weight-bearing, take first steps together. Give clear verbal cues ("Nice and slow," "Breathe normally").

Future arthroplasty: If the patient raises this, briefly normalise the discussion: "The team will talk with you about what options might be best for your joint in the long run, including possibly a knee replacement — called an arthroplasty. That conversation will come later; today is just about getting you moving."

Close: Praise the patient's effort, document weight-bearing achieved, plan the next session.
