Question 1 · Fill-in-the-blank THIS WEEK
Following his partial knee ___________, the physiotherapist noted significant intra-articular ___________ formation, which was limiting the patient's range of movement and causing recurrent joint stiffness.
A. thoracostomy … fibrosis
B. meniscectomy … adhesion
C. synovectomy … exostosis
D. chondroplasty … hyperinflation
Show Answer
B — meniscectomy … adhesion
A meniscectomy is the surgical removal of part or all of the meniscus of the knee. Post-operatively, adhesion formation (fibrous scar tissue binding previously mobile structures) is a recognised complication that restricts joint mobility. The other options combine unrelated procedural and pathological terms.
Question 2 · Fill-in-the-blank THIS WEEK
The patient described sharp, stabbing chest pain that worsened markedly on deep inspiration and coughing. This pattern is most consistent with ___________ chest pain and warrants further investigation for underlying pulmonary pathology.
A. cardiogenic
B. pleuritic
C. presyncope
D. mediastinal
Show Answer
B — pleuritic
Pleuritic chest pain is characteristically sharp, well-localised, and aggravated by inspiration, coughing, or movement. It indicates irritation of the parietal pleura, as seen in pleurisy, pulmonary embolism, or pneumonia. Option C (presyncope) relates to dizziness/near-fainting and is unrelated to this description.
Question 3 · Definition Match THIS WEEK
Which term describes impaired ability to perform rapid, rhythmic, alternating movements (such as forearm pronation and supination), indicative of cerebellar dysfunction?
A. hemineglect
B. meningism
C. dysdiadochokinesia
D. abulia
Show Answer
C — dysdiadochokinesia
Dysdiadochokinesia is tested clinically by asking the patient to alternately pronate and supinate the forearm rapidly (the diadochokinesia test). Impairment indicates cerebellar pathology, typically ipsilateral cerebellar lesions. Hemineglect = failure to attend to one side; meningism = signs of meningeal irritation; abulia = loss of motivation/initiative.
Question 4 · Fill-in-the-blank THIS WEEK
The chest X-ray of the patient with severe COPD demonstrated marked ___________ of the lungs, with flattened hemidiaphragms and an increased AP diameter, consistent with significant air trapping.
A. fibrosis
B. hyperinflation
C. mediastinum
D. consolidation
Show Answer
B — hyperinflation
Hyperinflation refers to abnormal over-distension of the lungs due to air trapping. On CXR, classic signs include flattened hemidiaphragms, an increased AP diameter ("barrel chest"), and hyperlucency of lung fields. It is a hallmark of obstructive lung disease such as COPD and severe asthma.
Question 5 · Definition Match THIS WEEK
Which term describes a benign, fluid-filled cystic swelling arising from a tendon sheath or joint capsule, most commonly presenting on the dorsum of the wrist?
A. exostosis
B. allograft
C. ganglion
D. haemarthrosis
Show Answer
C — ganglion
A ganglion is a soft, non-tender (or mildly tender) cystic lump filled with thick, gelatinous synovial fluid. It commonly arises on the dorsal wrist and can also occur at the ankle or foot. Exostosis is a bony outgrowth; allograft is donor tissue; haemarthrosis is blood within a joint cavity.
Question 6 · Collocation Complete THIS WEEK
Complete the clinical collocation: "___________ soft-tissue swelling" — indicating inflammation in the tissues immediately surrounding a joint.
A. intra-articular
B. periarticular
C. subchondral
D. interstitial
Show Answer
B — periarticular
Periarticular (peri = around, articular = joint) describes structures situated or occurring in the tissues immediately surrounding a joint. "Periarticular soft-tissue swelling" is a standard radiological and clinical descriptor. Intra-articular = inside the joint; subchondral = beneath cartilage; interstitial = within tissue spaces.
Question 7 · Fill-in-the-blank PREVIOUS
On examination following the rugby tackle, the knee was markedly swollen and tense. Aspiration revealed frank blood, confirming the clinical diagnosis of ___________, most likely secondary to anterior cruciate ligament rupture.
A. effusion
B. anasarca
C. haemarthrosis
D. tenderness
Show Answer
C — haemarthrosis
Haemarthrosis is the accumulation of blood within a joint cavity, most commonly resulting from trauma. Aspiration of frank blood from a tense knee following a significant mechanism of injury is highly indicative of haemarthrosis, with ACL rupture, peripheral meniscal tears, and fractures being the most common causes. A general effusion contains synovial fluid rather than blood.
Question 8 · Collocation Complete PREVIOUS
Complete the clinical collocation: "generalised ___________" — referring to severe, whole-body oedema involving the subcutaneous tissues.
A. presyncope
B. anasarca
C. coryza
D. purpura
Show Answer
B — anasarca
Anasarca specifically describes severe, generalised oedema involving subcutaneous tissues throughout the entire body. The phrase "generalised anasarca" is clinically redundant but used for emphasis. It indicates conditions such as end-stage heart failure, nephrotic syndrome, or hepatic failure. It is distinct from localised oedema (e.g. a single limb).
Question 9 · Definition Match PREVIOUS
Which term relates to the fibula or to the lateral compartment of the lower leg, particularly the nerve and muscles coursing around the fibular head?
A. cruciate
B. contralateral
C. peroneal
D. isometric
Show Answer
C — peroneal
Peroneal relates to the fibula (peroneus = fibula in Latin) or the lateral aspect of the lower leg. The common peroneal (fibular) nerve winds around the fibular head and is susceptible to injury there. Peroneal muscles (fibularis longus and brevis) evert the foot. Cruciate = cross-shaped knee ligaments; contralateral = opposite side; isometric = muscle contracts without joint movement.
Question 10 · Fill-in-the-blank PREVIOUS
The patient presented with right-sided hemiplegia following a left middle cerebral artery stroke. Neurological assessment revealed increased tone and hyperreflexia in the ___________ upper and lower limbs.
A. ipsilateral
B. bilateral
C. contralateral
D. unilateral
Show Answer
A — ipsilateral
With a left MCA stroke causing right-sided hemiplegia, the affected limbs are on the right side. In relation to the right-sided hemiplegia, the affected limbs are ipsilateral (same side as the hemiplegia). Note: if the question asked about the relationship to the stroke lesion, the answer would be contralateral (the motor decussation means left brain → right body). The key here is that "ipsilateral" is the correct answer for the affected side relative to the hemiplegia. (This is a deliberate nuance question — read the clinical context carefully in the OET.)

CLINICAL JOURNAL EXCERPT — Physiotherapy Management of Chronic Knee Conditions The conservative management of knee injuries requires a thorough understanding of the pathological processes involved. A lateral ankle sprain, whilst commonly regarded as a minor injury, may result in significant periarticular inflammation that extends to adjacent structures if inadequately treated. Similarly, the sequelae of knee trauma can be complex: an untreated ligamentous injury may predispose the patient to secondary conditions, including the development of a popliteal ganglion arising from the posterior joint capsule. In patients with a history of prior surgical intervention — particularly those who have undergone meniscectomy — physiotherapists must remain vigilant for signs of intra-articular adhesion formation. Such adhesions restrict passive and active range of movement, producing a characteristic end-feel of firm resistance and often accompanied by hypoaesthesia distal to the joint when scar tissue encroaches upon adjacent neurovascular structures. Assessment should incorporate both subjective and objective measures. Clinicians must distinguish between periarticular tenderness (suggestive of bursal or ligamentous irritation) and intra-articular pathology, as these presentations demand markedly different rehabilitation approaches. Where conservative treatment fails to restore adequate function, surgical review may be warranted to address underlying structural compromise.

Reading Question 4 · Inferential

Reading Question 5 · Vocabulary in Context
In the context of the passage, the word "periarticular" (used twice) most closely means:
A. within the joint space
B. beneath the articular cartilage
C. surrounding the joint and its immediate soft tissues
D. relating to the synovial membrane only
Show Answer
C — surrounding the joint and its immediate soft tissues
Periarticular = peri (around/surrounding) + articular (joint). It refers to the structures immediately surrounding a joint — including the joint capsule, adjacent ligaments, tendons, bursae, and soft tissue. This distinguishes it from intra-articular (inside the joint) and subchondral (beneath cartilage).

Role Play 1 — Neurological Physiotherapy

Setting: Neurology inpatient ward, Auckland City Hospital

Your role: Physiotherapist

Patient: Mrs Patricia Ngata, 54-year-old woman recovering from viral encephalitis. She was admitted 10 days ago with fever, altered consciousness, and signs of meningism. She is now medically stable but has residual neurological deficits including dysdiadochokinesia affecting her right upper limb, and reports fatigue and low mood.

Your task: Explain to Mrs Ngata what her key physiotherapy goals are for the next two weeks, address her concern that "my arm feels clumsy and won't do what I want", and outline what to expect in her rehabilitation journey. You must convey realistic expectations while maintaining a supportive, patient-centred approach.

Key vocabulary to use naturally:

encephalitis dysdiadochokinesia coordination exercises neuroplasticity gradual recovery

Suggested opening: "Mrs Ngata, thank you for speaking with me today. I understand you've been through quite a difficult time since your admission — the encephalitis has understandably left you feeling exhausted and frustrated with some of the changes in your movement…"

Show Model Answer Outline
Key communication points:

1. Acknowledge and validate — "It's completely understandable to feel frustrated. Your brain has been through a serious infection (encephalitis), and recovery takes time."

2. Explain dysdiadochokinesia simply — "The clumsiness in your right arm is something we call a coordination difficulty — your brain is having trouble sending smooth, rapid signals to the arm muscles. It's a recognised effect of the encephalitis on part of your brain called the cerebellum."

3. Outline physiotherapy goals — Improving coordination through repetitive, targeted exercises (e.g. alternating hand movements); building strength and endurance; addressing fatigue management.

4. Realistic expectations — "Recovery from encephalitis is often gradual and varies from person to person. Most people see continued improvement over weeks to months. Your brain has a remarkable ability to adapt and re-learn — this is called neuroplasticity."

5. Involve the patient — Ask about her goals: "What would you most like to be able to do again first?" This aligns rehabilitation with what matters most to her.

6. Safety netting — Advise on falls risk given coordination difficulty; ensure she understands who to contact if symptoms worsen.

Role Play 2 — Post-Operative Knee Rehabilitation

Setting: Outpatient physiotherapy clinic

Your role: Physiotherapist

Patient: Mr James Holley, 38-year-old recreational footballer, 8 weeks post-operative from cruciate ligament reconstruction and synovectomy (right knee). He has been attending weekly physiotherapy but is growing impatient, asking why he "can't just go back to playing football already" and expressing frustration about the slow pace of recovery.

Your task: Explain the process of biological remodelling and why the timeline for return to sport is evidence-based. Address his frustration empathetically, explain the risks of premature return, and motivate him to continue with his current programme.

Key vocabulary to use naturally:

cruciate reconstruction synovectomy remodelling phase ligament maturation return-to-sport criteria

Suggested opening: "James, I can really hear how frustrated you're feeling — eight weeks is a long time to be away from the sport you love. Let me explain what's actually happening inside your knee right now, because I think it'll help make sense of why we're taking this timeline seriously…"

Show Model Answer Outline
Key communication points:

1. Empathy first — "I completely understand your frustration. You're clearly motivated and you feel physically better — and that's actually a great sign."

2. Explain remodelling — "Here's the thing: even though the outside of your knee feels stronger, the graft inside is still going through a process called remodelling. After your cruciate reconstruction, the graft ligament goes through phases — it actually gets weaker before it gets stronger, and it takes around 9–12 months to fully mature. At 8 weeks, we're still in a vulnerable phase."

3. Explain synovectomy recovery — "You also had a synovectomy — removal of the inflamed joint lining — so your knee needs extra time for the remaining synovium to settle and reduce its inflammatory response before high-impact loading."

4. Risk of premature return — "Returning to football before the remodelling is complete significantly increases the risk of re-rupture — and that would mean surgery again, with a longer recovery."

5. Return-to-sport criteria — "We use objective tests — strength ratios, hop tests, psychological readiness — to decide when it's safe. You're doing really well; let's aim for full return at around 9 months."

6. Motivate and goal-set — "What can you do right now? Non-contact training, ball skills, conditioning. Let's set some milestones you can celebrate on the way."
