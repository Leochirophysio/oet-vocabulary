⏱ 15 min
Question 1 · Fill-in-the-blank THIS WEEK — stridor (25 May)
During the respiratory assessment, the physiotherapist noted a high-pitched, harsh ________ sound on inspiration, indicating partial upper airway obstruction secondary to laryngeal oedema.
A) rhonchi
B) stridor
C) wheeze
D) murmur
Show Answer
B) stridor. Stridor is a high-pitched, monophonic sound heard on inspiration (or both phases) caused by turbulent airflow through a narrowed upper airway. It differs from wheeze (lower airway, expiratory) and rhonchi (secretion-related, coarse sounds in larger airways).
Question 2 · Fill-in-the-blank THIS WEEK — ventilation (25 May)
Incentive spirometry was prescribed post-operatively to promote deep breathing, improve ________, and reduce the risk of basal atelectasis in this high-risk patient.
A) defibrillation
B) secretion clearance
C) ventilation
D) perfusion
Show Answer
C) ventilation. Ventilation refers to the mechanical process of moving air into and out of the lungs. Incentive spirometry specifically targets alveolar ventilation by encouraging sustained maximal inspiration, which helps prevent collapse of lung segments post-operatively.
Question 3 · Definition Match THIS WEEK — confabulation (29 May)
Which of the following best describes confabulation?
A) The inability to perform purposeful movements despite intact motor function and comprehension
B) The fabrication of detailed but false memories without conscious intent to deceive, commonly associated with frontal lobe or Korsakoff's pathology
C) The failure to recognise familiar objects, faces, or sounds despite intact primary sensory function
D) The loss of voluntary initiation of movement in the absence of paralysis
Show Answer
B) Confabulation is the spontaneous production of fabricated, distorted, or misinterpreted memories about oneself or the world, without the intention to deceive. It is commonly seen in Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome, TBI, and some dementias. Compare: apraxia (A), agnosia (C), akinesia (D).
Question 4 · Fill-in-the-blank THIS WEEK — nociceptive (28 May)
The patient's knee pain was classified as ________ in origin, arising from structural damage to the medial meniscus following a twisting injury, with no evidence of nerve root compromise.
A) discogenic
B) neurogenic
C) nociceptive
D) referred
Show Answer
C) nociceptive. Nociceptive pain arises from actual or threatened tissue damage, activating specialised peripheral pain receptors (nociceptors). It is localised and proportionate to injury. Neurogenic/neuropathic pain (B) involves nerve dysfunction; discogenic (A) is a subtype of nociceptive pain specific to intervertebral disc pathology.
Question 5 · Definition Match THIS WEEK — lymphoedema (31 May)
Which of the following best describes lymphoedema?
A) Bilateral lower limb swelling due to right-sided cardiac failure and venous hypertension
B) Chronic swelling of one or more limbs caused by impaired lymphatic drainage, resulting in protein-rich fluid accumulation in the interstitium
C) Acute inflammatory joint swelling with erythema and warmth secondary to gout or septic arthritis
D) Transient peripheral oedema caused by prolonged immobility or dependent positioning
Show Answer
B) lymphoedema is chronic swelling resulting from lymphatic insufficiency. Unlike cardiac oedema (A) or dependent oedema (D), lymphoedema involves protein-rich fluid that leads to progressive tissue fibrosis and induration if untreated. It is commonly secondary to axillary node dissection, radiotherapy, or infection (filariasis).
Question 6 · Collocation Complete THIS WEEK — ankylosis (31 May)
The radiograph confirmed complete bony ________ of the proximal interphalangeal joint, with loss of the joint space and osseous fusion of the articular surfaces following chronic inflammatory arthropathy.
A) contracture
B) rigidity
C) ankylosis
D) arthralgia
Show Answer
C) ankylosis is the pathological stiffening or immobility of a joint, either through bony (true/osseous ankylosis) or fibrous fusion. It is the hallmark feature of advanced ankylosing spondylitis and certain post-traumatic or post-infective joint conditions. Contracture (A) involves soft tissue shortening without osseous union; rigidity (B) is a neurological sign of increased muscle tone.
Question 7 · Fill-in-the-blank PREVIOUS — dysphagia
Following the patient's brainstem stroke, the speech pathologist confirmed severe ________, with unsafe swallowing of all consistencies and high risk of aspiration pneumonia requiring nil-by-mouth status and enteral feeding.
A) dysphonia
B) dysarthria
C) dysphagia
D) dyspnoea
Show Answer
C) dysphagia is difficulty swallowing, commonly resulting from neurological lesions affecting the swallowing centres in the brainstem. Dysphonia (A) is impaired voice quality; dysarthria (B) is impaired motor speech articulation; dyspnoea (D) is breathlessness. All four share the prefix dys- (difficulty/impairment) — context and the associated organ system distinguish them.
Question 8 · Fill-in-the-blank PREVIOUS — spasticity
The upper motor neurone lesion resulted in marked ________ of the right lower limb, characterised by velocity-dependent increased muscle tone and a clasp-knife response on rapid passive dorsiflexion of the ankle.
A) flaccidity
B) spasticity
C) hypotonia
D) atrophy
Show Answer
B) spasticity is a feature of upper motor neurone (UMN) syndrome characterised by velocity-dependent increased muscle tone. This distinguishes it from rigidity (lead-pipe or cogwheel, seen in Parkinson's disease) which is not velocity-dependent. Flaccidity (A) and hypotonia (C) occur in lower motor neurone lesions. The clasp-knife response is pathognomonic of spasticity.
Question 9 · Definition Match PREVIOUS — proprioception
Which of the following best describes proprioception?
A) The conscious awareness of pain and temperature arising from peripheral nociceptors
B) The sense of body position, movement, and spatial orientation provided by mechanoreceptors in muscles, tendons, and joint capsules
C) The ability to modulate neural pathways in response to training or injury (neural adaptability)
D) The perception of vibration transmitted via large-diameter myelinated A-beta fibres
Show Answer
B) proprioception is the body's intrinsic sense of its own position and movement. Key receptors include muscle spindles (Ia & II afferents), Golgi tendon organs (Ib), and joint mechanoreceptors. Proprioception is commonly impaired in peripheral neuropathy, ligamentous injury, and following joint replacement, making balance and coordination training a critical component of physiotherapy rehabilitation.
Question 10 · Collocation Complete PREVIOUS — atelectasis
Post-operative chest X-ray revealed basal ________ of the left lower lobe, prompting urgent physiotherapy referral for airway clearance techniques and breathing exercises.
A) consolidation
B) effusion
C) atelectasis
D) pneumothorax
Show Answer
C) atelectasis is the collapse or incomplete expansion of lung tissue, commonly seen post-operatively due to shallow breathing, mucus plugging, or compression. Physiotherapy is first-line management. Consolidation (A) involves alveolar filling with fluid/pus (e.g. pneumonia); effusion (B) is fluid in the pleural space; pneumothorax (D) is air in the pleural space.

CLINICAL CASE SUMMARY — Respiratory & Musculoskeletal Co-presentation Mrs Eleanor Hart, a 52-year-old secondary school teacher, was admitted to the respiratory ward at Auckland City Hospital following an acute exacerbation of asthma complicated by bilateral lower lobe pneumonia. On initial physiotherapy assessment, an audible wheeze was noted on expiration bilaterally, and the patient had copious respiratory secretions she was unable to clear independently. Active cycle of breathing techniques combined with postural drainage were commenced to support ventilation and facilitate secretion clearance, with significant improvement noted within 48 hours.

Alongside her respiratory presentation, Mrs Hart described a three-month history of right-sided sciatica, with lancinating pain radiating from the buttock to the dorsum of the foot. The pain was consistent with neurogenic irritation at the L5 nerve root, exacerbated by the Valsalva manoeuvre during coughing episodes. The treating physiotherapist demonstrated considerable empathy throughout the assessment, acknowledging the patient's distress and the significant impact of her dual diagnosis on her professional and personal life.

A comprehensive rehabilitation programme was developed to address both presentations concurrently, incorporating breathing retraining, graduated mobilisation, and neural tension management strategies tailored to Mrs Hart's functional capacity and pain tolerance.
Reading Question 1 · Factual
Which physiotherapy techniques were used to manage Mrs Hart's respiratory secretions?
A) Nebulisation with hypertonic saline and chest percussion
B) Active cycle of breathing techniques and postural drainage
C) Pursed-lip breathing and incentive spirometry
D) Intermittent positive pressure breathing and suctioning
Show Answer
B) The passage explicitly states: "Active cycle of breathing techniques combined with postural drainage were commenced to support ventilation and facilitate secretion clearance." The other options are valid physiotherapy interventions but are not mentioned in the passage.
Reading Question 2 · Factual
At which spinal level was Mrs Hart's nerve root irritation consistent?
A) L3/L4
B) L4/L5
C) L5
D) S1
Show Answer
C) L5. The passage states the pain was "consistent with neurogenic irritation at the L5 nerve root." Pain radiating from the buttock to the dorsum of the foot is a classic L5 dermatomal distribution, distinguishing it from S1 (lateral foot/heel) or L4 (medial shin/foot) patterns.
Reading Question 3 · Inferential
Why would coughing episodes be expected to aggravate Mrs Hart's sciatic pain?
A) Coughing causes direct compression of lumbar vertebrae through axial loading
B) Coughing triggers a Valsalva manoeuvre that transiently raises intrathecal pressure, increasing mechanical stress on the irritated nerve root
C) Coughing causes involuntary hip flexion that stretches the sciatic nerve
D) Coughing reduces perfusion to the lumbar nerve roots via arterial compression
Show Answer
B) The passage notes the pain was "exacerbated by the Valsalva manoeuvre during coughing episodes." Forced expiration against a closed glottis (Valsalva) raises intra-abdominal and intrathecal pressure, which in the presence of disc herniation or nerve root inflammation temporarily increases mechanical irritation of the compressed nerve root — a key clinical sign of discogenic or neurogenic pathology.
Reading Question 4 · Inferential
What does the physiotherapist's demonstration of empathy in this case primarily reflect?
A) A requirement to complete formal psychological screening tools
B) Acknowledgement that the patient's psychosocial context is clinically relevant and influences rehabilitation outcomes
C) A sign that the physiotherapist was uncertain about the management plan
D) A legal obligation to document the patient's emotional state
Show Answer
B) In OET and clinical practice, empathy is a cornerstone of patient-centred care. By acknowledging "the significant impact of her dual diagnosis on her professional and personal life," the physiotherapist recognises that psychosocial factors — including fear-avoidance, work-related anxiety, and identity — directly influence engagement with rehabilitation and functional outcomes.
Reading Question 5 · Vocabulary in Context
In the passage, the term neurogenic most closely means:
A) Arising from structural damage to bone or cartilage
B) Originating from or caused by dysfunction or irritation of the nervous system
C) Related to a psychological or somatic response without identifiable tissue pathology
D) Caused by impaired vascular supply to the peripheral nerves
Show Answer
B) In context, neurogenic describes pain arising from irritation or dysfunction of the nervous system — specifically the L5 nerve root. This distinguishes it from nociceptive pain (tissue damage, option A) or psychogenic pain (option C). Option D describes ischaemic neuropathy, which is a specific subtype. In clinical OET documentation, "neurogenic pain" specifically indicates neural tissue involvement.

Role Play 1 — Outpatient Physiotherapy Clinic, Auckland

Setting: You are a physiotherapist in an outpatient clinic. Your patient, Mrs Wei Chen (45F), has been referred with right lateral epicondylitis of six weeks' duration. Conservative management including exercises has produced only partial relief. You are recommending a course of iontophoresis.

Your task: Explain what iontophoresis is and why you are recommending it for her epicondylitis, describe what she can expect during treatment, and address her concern that she has heard "electrical treatments can be painful."

Target vocabulary to use: iontophoresis epicondylitis nociceptive

Suggested opening: "Mrs Chen, thank you for coming in today. I've reviewed your progress and I'd like to discuss an additional treatment option that I think could be very helpful — it's called iontophoresis. Have you come across this before?"

"Your elbow pain is due to a condition called lateral epicondylitis — sometimes called tennis elbow..."
"Iontophoresis uses a mild, low-level electrical current to drive anti-inflammatory medication directly into the tendon..."
"The pain is nociceptive in nature — it's coming from the tendon tissue itself..."
"You may feel a gentle tingling sensation, but the current is very low and should not be uncomfortable..."
"Most patients notice significant improvement after four to six sessions..."
Show Model Answer Outline
Model answer outline:
1. Greet and establish rapport — use the patient's name, acknowledge her ongoing discomfort.
2. Explain the diagnosis — describe lateral epicondylitis in lay terms: overuse injury to the tendon attaching the forearm extensor muscles to the lateral epicondyle of the humerus. Avoid excessive jargon; check understanding.
3. Explain iontophoresis — a non-invasive electrotherapy technique using a weak direct electrical current to deliver ionised medication (typically corticosteroid or anti-inflammatory agent) transdermally into the target tissue. Explain it does not involve needles or shock.
4. Explain the pain rationale — the pain is nociceptive, arising from localised tissue inflammation; iontophoresis targets this by reducing inflammation directly at the site.
5. Address the concern — reassure that the current used is very low (microamperes), the sensation is a mild tingling, and the treatment is stopped immediately if discomfort occurs. Many patients find it relaxing.
6. Check understanding and gain consent — ask if she has any questions, confirm she is happy to proceed, and outline the plan (frequency, number of sessions, expected outcome).

Role Play 2 — Community Physiotherapy Centre, Christchurch

Setting: You are a physiotherapist in a community clinic. Mr David Rangi (38M) has been referred by his GP for physiotherapy management of fibromyalgia. He arrives reluctant and frustrated, stating: "I've tried exercise before and it just makes everything worse — I don't see the point."

Your task: Acknowledge his frustration with empathy, explain the evidence base for graded exercise in fibromyalgia management, and discuss ergonomic modifications to his daily routine that may reduce his overall pain burden. Agree on a shared plan.

Target vocabulary to use: fibromyalgia ergonomic empathy

Suggested opening: "Mr Rangi, I really appreciate you coming in today — I can hear that you've had a difficult time with this, and I want to start by saying that your concerns make complete sense. Could you tell me a bit more about what happened when you tried exercising before?"

"Fibromyalgia causes widespread pain and fatigue, which can make exercise feel counterintuitive..."
"What you experienced is called post-exertional discomfort — it's very common and we can work around it..."
"The research consistently shows that a gentle, graded programme reduces pain over time..."
"Making ergonomic adjustments — like your workstation set-up and how you carry loads — can significantly reduce your daily pain..."
"We'll start very gently — even five minutes of walking — and increase only when you feel ready..."
"I want us to set goals together so this programme feels achievable, not overwhelming..."
Show Model Answer Outline
Model answer outline:
1. Open with empathy — validate his experience directly; do not dismiss or minimise his concerns. Use empathic language: "That sounds really difficult," "It makes sense you'd feel that way."
2. Explore his previous experience — ask open questions about what type of exercise he tried, at what intensity, and what the aftermath felt like. Normalise post-exertional discomfort as a known feature of fibromyalgia rather than a sign of harm.
3. Explain the evidence — graded aerobic exercise and resistance training are first-line non-pharmacological treatments for fibromyalgia. The key is starting below his symptom threshold and progressing slowly; aggressive early exercise is contraindicated.
4. Discuss ergonomic modifications — explain that ergonomic changes to workstation height, seating, load distribution, and sleep posture reduce cumulative physical strain and can meaningfully lower his baseline pain level, making exercise more accessible.
5. Collaborative goal-setting — negotiate a starting point he feels confident about (e.g. 5-minute daily walks), agree on a review point, and emphasise that the programme is entirely adjustable based on his response.
6. Close positively — thank him for engaging, affirm his autonomy, and provide written information about fibromyalgia self-management to reinforce the session.
