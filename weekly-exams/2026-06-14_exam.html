Q1 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK cardiomyopathy (10 Jun)
The cardiologist confirmed a diagnosis of dilated ___, explaining to the patient that the ventricles were enlarged and contracting with significantly reduced efficiency.
A. pericarditis
B. arrhythmia
C. cardiomyopathy
D. cardiomegaly
Show Answer
Correct answer: C — cardiomyopathy
Cardiomyopathy is disease of the heart muscle itself. Dilated cardiomyopathy specifically describes enlargement of the cardiac chambers with impaired systolic function. Cardiomegaly (D) refers to an enlarged heart on imaging but is a sign, not a diagnosis; pericarditis (A) is inflammation of the pericardium; arrhythmia (B) is a disorder of cardiac rhythm.
Q2 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK deconditioning (14 Jun)
After three weeks of hospitalisation and enforced bed rest, Mr Singh demonstrated signs of profound physical ___, including bilateral proximal muscle weakness and a markedly reduced tolerance to any form of exercise.
A. spasticity
B. atrophy
C. dystonia
D. deconditioning
Show Answer
Correct answer: D — deconditioning
Deconditioning describes the multi-system physiological deterioration resulting from prolonged immobility or reduced physical activity, encompassing cardiovascular, musculoskeletal and respiratory systems. Atrophy (B) refers specifically to muscle wasting; spasticity (A) is an upper motor neurone sign; dystonia (C) is an involuntary movement disorder.
Q3 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK diuresis (10 Jun)
The patient was commenced on high-dose intravenous furosemide to promote ___ and reduce the fluid overload that had developed in the context of her decompensated heart failure.
A. haemostasis
B. diuresis
C. ascites
D. perfusion
Show Answer
Correct answer: B — diuresis
Diuresis is the increased production and excretion of urine. Loop diuretics such as furosemide act on the loop of Henle to inhibit sodium and water reabsorption, thereby promoting diuresis and reducing preload. Ascites (C) is peritoneal fluid accumulation — a consequence of fluid overload, not a treatment.
Q4 · Fill-in-the-blank · PREVIOUS crepitus (20 Apr)
On passive flexion and extension of the left knee, the physiotherapist noted palpable and audible ___, which the patient described as a grinding sensation, consistent with degenerative articular cartilage changes.
A. effusion
B. atrophy
C. crepitus
D. contracture
Show Answer
Correct answer: C — crepitus
Crepitus is the crackling, grating or popping sensation perceived on palpation or auscultation during joint movement. It is commonly caused by roughened articular surfaces, bony changes or gas within a joint. Effusion (A) is fluid within a joint; contracture (D) is fixed shortening of soft tissue.
Q5 · Fill-in-the-blank · PREVIOUS atelectasis (2 May)
The post-operative chest radiograph demonstrated bibasal ___ in both lower lobes, attributed to inadequate tidal volume and diaphragmatic splinting following upper abdominal surgery.
A. consolidation
B. haemothorax
C. emphysema
D. atelectasis
Show Answer
Correct answer: D — atelectasis
Atelectasis is partial or complete collapse of lung segments, a common post-operative complication due to shallow breathing, mucus plugging or extrinsic compression. Consolidation (A) implies alveolar filling (e.g. pneumonia) rather than collapse; haemothorax (B) is blood in the pleural space; emphysema (C) is permanent alveolar destruction.
Q6 · Definition match · THIS WEEK synovial (9 Jun)
Which term best describes: "Relating to the viscous lubricating fluid and the membrane that produces it, found lining the inner surface of joint capsules and tendon sheaths"?
A. osseous
B. periosteal
C. synovial
D. fascial
Show Answer
Correct answer: C — synovial
Synovial structures relate to the synovium. Synovial fluid (synovia) is secreted by the synovial membrane to lubricate articulating surfaces and nourish avascular cartilage. Osseous (A) = relating to bone; periosteal (B) = relating to the periosteum; fascial (D) = relating to fascia.
Q7 · Definition match · THIS WEEK hyperaesthesia (12 Jun)
Which term describes: "An abnormal increase in sensory sensitivity, particularly to touch or pain stimuli, in which ordinarily innocuous sensations are perceived as unusually intense or distressing"?
A. allodynia
B. paraesthesia
C. nociception
D. hyperaesthesia
Show Answer
Correct answer: D — hyperaesthesia
Hyperaesthesia is exaggerated or heightened sensitivity to sensory stimulation. It differs from allodynia (A), where a normally non-painful stimulus causes pain; and from paraesthesia (B), which is an abnormal spontaneous sensation (e.g. tingling) without a stimulus. Nociception (C) is simply the neural process of encoding noxious stimuli.
Q8 · Definition match · PREVIOUS spasticity (29 Apr)
Which term refers to: "A velocity-dependent increase in muscle tone resulting from an upper motor neurone lesion, characterised by a sudden catch or release during passive muscle stretch"?
A. flaccidity
B. hypertonia
C. rigidity
D. spasticity
Show Answer
Correct answer: D — spasticity
The hallmark of spasticity is its velocity-dependence — resistance increases with the speed of passive stretch. Rigidity (C) shows constant resistance throughout the range regardless of speed (as in Parkinson's disease). Hypertonia (B) is a broader term encompassing any increase in muscle tone. Flaccidity (A) is decreased tone, seen in lower motor neurone lesions.
Q9 · Collocation complete · THIS WEEK callus (11 Jun)
Bony ___ formation was evident on the four-week follow-up X-ray, bridging the fracture gap and indicating that the healing process was progressing as expected.
A. periosteum
B. callus
C. labrum
D. avulsion
Show Answer
Correct answer: B — callus
Fracture callus (bony callus) is the provisional healing tissue that forms at a fracture site during the reparative phase. It begins as a soft fibrocartilaginous callus and progressively mineralises into hard bone. The periosteum (A) is the membrane surrounding bone; a labrum (C) is a fibrocartilaginous rim around a joint socket; avulsion (D) is a fracture where bone is pulled away by a tendon or ligament.
Q10 · Collocation complete · PREVIOUS oedema (16 Apr)
On examination, bilateral pitting ___ was documented to the mid-shin bilaterally, indicating significant fluid accumulation in the interstitial tissue of the lower limbs.
A. effusion
B. ascites
C. diuresis
D. oedema
Show Answer
Correct answer: D — oedema
"Pitting oedema" is the clinical term for soft-tissue swelling that retains a temporary indentation when firm pressure is applied, indicating fluid in the interstitial space. Effusion (A) specifically refers to fluid within a joint or body cavity; ascites (B) is peritoneal fluid accumulation; diuresis (C) is the therapeutic process of promoting urine excretion.

Management of Decompensated Heart Failure in a Community Hospital Setting

A 67-year-old male with a longstanding background of ischaemic cardiomyopathy was admitted to the cardiology ward following a three-day history of worsening exertional breathlessness and progressive bilateral lower-limb swelling. On admission, clinical examination revealed bilateral basal crackles consistent with pulmonary oedema, and alveolar flooding was evident on the chest radiograph, appearing as diffuse opacification of both lower zones.

Abdominal assessment demonstrated distension with shifting dullness on percussion, consistent with ascites, which was subsequently confirmed by bedside ultrasound revealing significant free fluid within the peritoneal cavity. Initial blood tests demonstrated a haemoglobin of 78 g/L, confirming severe anaemia, likely secondary to chronic disease and reduced erythropoietin production in the context of concomitant renal impairment. The renal profile also highlighted an acute kidney injury (creatinine 245 µmol/L), which complicated the titration of diuretic therapy.

The treating cardiologist commenced high-dose intravenous loop diuretics to promote diuresis and reduce cardiac preload. Strict fluid balance charts were maintained alongside daily weight measurements to monitor treatment response. The inpatient physiotherapy team was involved from day two, providing low-intensity respiratory techniques to maintain ventilation and prevent further lung collapse. By day five, the patient had lost 4.2 kg of excess fluid, breathlessness had improved substantially, and oxygen saturation had risen from 88% to 95% on 2 L per minute of supplemental oxygen.

Reading Comprehension Questions
Reading Q1 · Factual
How was the presence of ascites confirmed in this patient?
A. Chest radiography
B. Blood tests showing elevated creatinine
C. Bedside ultrasound revealing free peritoneal fluid
D. Abdominal percussion alone
Show Answer
Correct answer: C
The passage states: "which was subsequently confirmed by bedside ultrasound revealing significant free fluid within the peritoneal cavity." Shifting dullness on percussion raised the clinical suspicion, but the diagnosis was confirmed by ultrasound imaging.
Reading Q2 · Factual
What was the patient's haemoglobin level on admission, and how was this classified?
A. 88 g/L — moderate anaemia
B. 78 g/L — severe anaemia
C. 245 g/L — polycythaemia
D. 78 g/L — mild anaemia
Show Answer
Correct answer: B
The passage states: "a haemoglobin of 78 g/L, confirming severe anaemia." Note that 245 is the creatinine level (renal function marker), not the haemoglobin.
Reading Q3 · Inferential
Why was diuretic management described as "complicated" in this patient?
A. The patient refused intravenous medication
B. Liver failure impaired drug metabolism
C. A concomitant acute kidney injury made titration difficult
D. The ascites prevented adequate drug absorption
Show Answer
Correct answer: C
The passage notes "acute kidney injury (creatinine 245 µmol/L), which complicated the titration of diuretic therapy." Diuretics can exacerbate renal impairment by reducing glomerular filtration pressure, so their use must be carefully balanced against worsening kidney function.
Reading Q4 · Inferential
What was the primary clinical purpose of the physiotherapy team's involvement from day two?
A. To facilitate early discharge planning
B. To maintain ventilation and prevent lung collapse
C. To manage the patient's lower-limb oedema with compression
D. To monitor daily weight and fluid balance
Show Answer
Correct answer: B
The passage states physiotherapy was involved "providing low-intensity respiratory techniques to maintain ventilation and prevent further lung collapse." The term "lung collapse" corresponds to atelectasis, a common complication in bed-bound patients with pulmonary oedema.
Reading Q5 · Vocabulary in context
In this passage, the term alveolar (paragraph 1) most closely relates to which anatomical structure?
A. The peritoneal cavity
B. The cardiac chambers
C. The air sacs within the lungs responsible for gas exchange
D. The renal tubules
Show Answer
Correct answer: C
"Alveolar" relates to the alveoli — the microscopic air sacs at the terminal branches of the bronchial tree where gas exchange occurs. "Alveolar flooding" describes the accumulation of fluid within these air sacs, resulting in impaired oxygenation, as seen in pulmonary oedema.

Show Self-Assessment Checklist (open AFTER completing your letter)

Writing Self-Assessment — 10 Criteria

  • Opening sentence clearly identifies patient (name, DOB) and states the purpose of the referral without unnecessary preamble.
  • Diagnosis and surgical procedure are accurately named (right hip labral tear; arthroscopic labral repair).
  • Key operative findings are summarised: osseous impingement, avascular changes to femoral head, bony callus at stress fracture site.
  • Post-operative course is described concisely, including weight-bearing progression and current pain level.
  • Current functional limitations are stated with objective data (ROM figures and MRC strength grade) — not vague generalisations.
  • Deconditioning secondary to prolonged activity restriction is mentioned as a significant rehabilitation consideration.
  • Patient's personal goals and emotional concerns (return to running, anxiety re timeline) are acknowledged in the letter.
  • The specific request is explicit — e.g. "I would be grateful if you could arrange a post-surgical rehabilitation programme" — rather than implied.
  • Formal OET register is maintained throughout: no colloquial language, no first-person patient voice, appropriate hedging language (e.g. "I would appreciate…").
  • Letter is appropriately formatted: correct addressee, professional salutation, signatory block, and word count is approximately 180–200 words.

Role Play 1 — Post-Hip Labral Repair Patient Education

Setting: Outpatient physiotherapy clinic, AUT Millennium Institute. Day of first post-discharge appointment.

Roles: You are the physiotherapist. The interlocutor plays Mrs Amelia Parata (53 years), who had a right hip arthroscopic labral repair 12 days ago and has just been discharged from hospital.

Key tasks:

  • Explain what deconditioning means and why it has occurred
  • Reassure her about the mild avascular changes noted to her femoral head
  • Describe the initial rehabilitation goals and precautions for the first two weeks
  • Address her concerns about returning to running

Vocabulary to use: deconditioning   avascular   spasm

Suggested opening: "Good morning, Mrs Parata. It's great to meet you in the outpatient setting. How have you been feeling since you went home? … Thank you for letting me know. Today I'd like to start by explaining what to expect over the next few weeks of your recovery…"

"Because of the time you've spent resting, you've developed some deconditioning…"
"The surgeon noted some avascular changes, which means the blood supply to part of the bone needs monitoring…"
"The muscle spasm you're feeling is quite normal at this stage…"
"We'll progress your exercises gradually — your body needs time to adapt…"
"Most patients with a similar repair return to running between four and six months — we'll get you there safely."
Show Model Answer Outline — Role Play 1
Model structure for Role Play 1:

1. Build rapport & elicit concerns (30 sec): Welcome, open question about how she has been managing at home, acknowledge her anxiety about recovery.

2. Explain deconditioning (60 sec): "Because of the six weeks of restricted activity before surgery, and then twelve days in hospital, your body has undergone what we call deconditioning — this means your muscles, cardiovascular system and overall fitness have all reduced. It's completely expected, and it's one of the main things we'll address in rehabilitation."

3. Address avascular changes (45 sec): "Mr Rajan noted some mild avascular changes to your femoral head — avascular means the blood supply to a small area of bone was slightly reduced. This is Grade One, which is the mildest level, and is being monitored. It means we need to be careful with high-impact loading initially, which is another reason we won't rush straight back to running."

4. Reassure about muscle spasm (30 sec): "The spasm you're feeling around the hip is your muscles reacting to the surgery — it's a protective response and will settle as the tissues heal and you begin gentle movement."

5. Outline initial rehabilitation plan (60 sec): Range of motion exercises, gentle hip muscle activation, hydrotherapy if available, gradual weight-bearing progression, no pivoting or high-impact for 12 weeks.

6. Respond to running concern (30 sec): Validate her goal, provide realistic timeline (4–6 months), explain that safe progression reduces re-injury risk.

Role Play 2 — Parent Education: Paediatric Hypertonia

Setting: Community paediatric physiotherapy clinic. Initial assessment appointment.

Roles: You are the paediatric physiotherapist. The interlocutor plays Ms Rosa Kim, mother of Liam (18 months), who has been referred by the paediatrician following concerns about increased muscle tone in his lower limbs. The mother appears anxious and has not received a clear explanation from the referring doctor.

Key tasks:

  • Explain what hypertonia means in plain language
  • Describe the physiotherapy assessment you have completed and your initial findings
  • Outline the management plan, including what to watch for if treatment is initially ineffective
  • Address the mother's concern about whether Liam will walk normally

Vocabulary to use: hypertonia   refractory   somnolence

Suggested opening: "Hello, Ms Kim. Thank you for coming in today. I understand this has been a worrying time. I've had a chance to review Liam's referral and spend some time assessing him this morning — would it be helpful if I explained what I've found and what we're planning to do?"

"Hypertonia means that the muscles in Liam's legs have a higher-than-normal resting tone…"
"We'll start with stretching and movement exercises — most children respond well to this…"
"If his tone proves refractory to physiotherapy — that is, if it doesn't respond as we hope — we may involve the paediatrician to consider other options…"
"One thing to watch for at home is unusual sleepiness or somnolence, which can sometimes be a sign that we need to reassess…"
"Many children with Liam's presentation do learn to walk — our goal is to give his development the best possible support."
Show Model Answer Outline — Role Play 2
Model structure for Role Play 2:

1. Acknowledge concerns & gain consent to explain (30 sec): Empathise with mother's anxiety, check she is comfortable to hear the findings, use inclusive language ("we" / "together").

2. Explain hypertonia in plain language (60 sec): "Hypertonia means that Liam's muscles are working at a higher level of tension than would be typical for his age. Think of it like a rubber band that's being held a little too tight. This makes it harder for him to move his legs freely and can affect how he stands and eventually walks. It doesn't mean he can't get better — it means we need to work on relaxing and stretching those muscles."

3. Share assessment findings (45 sec): Describe tone assessment results, developmental milestones, any asymmetry noted, and that you've ruled out pain as a contributing factor.

4. Outline management plan (60 sec): Weekly physiotherapy sessions, home stretching programme taught to parent, positioning strategies, developmental play activities. Emphasise parent involvement as key.

5. Explain what to do if refractory (30 sec): "If Liam's tone proves refractory — meaning it doesn't improve with our physiotherapy — we won't simply continue the same approach. I'll refer back to the paediatrician, who may consider other assessments or treatments such as oral medication or specialist review."

6. Somnolence & red flags (30 sec): "At home, please contact us or your GP if you notice unusual somnolence — excessive sleepiness or difficulty rousing Liam — or any changes in his feeding or breathing, as these can sometimes indicate the need for urgent reassessment."

7. Address walking concern & close (30 sec): Validate the question honestly, avoid making firm promises, emphasise early intervention improves outcomes, invite further questions.
