Q1 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK quadriceps (21 Jun · Musculoskeletal)
Following anterior knee surgery, the patient demonstrated significant weakness of the ___ muscle group, with particular atrophy noted in the vastus medialis oblique. The treating physiotherapist prescribed targeted strengthening exercises to restore full knee extension power.
A) hamstrings
B) quadriceps
C) gastrocnemius
D) tibialis anterior
Show Answer
B) quadriceps — The quadriceps muscle group, comprising the four muscles on the anterior thigh (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, and vastus intermedius), is the primary knee extensor and is commonly weakened following knee surgery or prolonged immobilisation.
Q2 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK thermotherapy (18 Jun · Procedures)
The physiotherapist applied ___ in the form of a heated wheat bag to the patient's lower back for 15 minutes prior to commencing soft tissue mobilisation, aiming to reduce muscle guarding and improve tissue extensibility.
A) cryotherapy
B) hydrotherapy
C) thermotherapy
D) electrotherapy
Show Answer
C) thermotherapy — Thermotherapy is the therapeutic application of heat to tissues. It promotes vasodilatation, reduces muscle spasm, and increases collagen extensibility, making it useful as a preparatory modality before manual therapy or exercise.
Q3 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK orthostatic (17 Jun · Cardiopulmonary)
Following six days of bed rest post-operatively, the patient reported dizziness on rising from a sitting position. Blood pressure monitoring confirmed a drop of 28 mmHg in systolic pressure on standing, consistent with ___ hypotension.
A) systolic
B) diastolic
C) orthostatic
D) neurogenic
Show Answer
C) orthostatic — Orthostatic (postural) hypotension is defined as a sustained reduction in systolic blood pressure of ≥ 20 mmHg or diastolic BP of ≥ 10 mmHg within three minutes of assuming an upright position. It is a recognised risk during early mobilisation following prolonged bed rest.
Q4 · Fill-in-the-blank · THIS WEEK hypoventilation (15 Jun · Cardiopulmonary)
Post-operative monitoring revealed ___, with the patient's respiratory rate falling to 7 breaths per minute and SpO₂ declining to 89%. The recovery nurse administered supplemental oxygen via Hudson mask and alerted the on-call registrar.
A) hyperventilation
B) hypoventilation
C) tachypnoea
D) apnoea
Show Answer
B) hypoventilation — Hypoventilation refers to an inadequate rate or depth of breathing, resulting in insufficient alveolar gas exchange, carbon dioxide retention (hypercapnia), and hypoxaemia. It is distinct from apnoea (complete cessation of breathing) and commonly occurs as a side effect of opioid analgesia post-operatively.
Q5 · Fill-in-the-blank · REVIEW deconditioning (14 Jun · Signs & Symptoms)
Following a prolonged hospitalisation of seven weeks, Mr Tane presented with marked ___, characterised by reduced exercise tolerance, generalised muscle weakness, decreased bone density, and poor cardiovascular endurance — all attributable to sustained physical inactivity.
A) sarcopaenia
B) malnutrition
C) deconditioning
D) fatigue syndrome
Show Answer
C) deconditioning — Deconditioning is the physiological deterioration across multiple organ systems resulting from prolonged inactivity or bed rest. It differs from sarcopaenia (age-related muscle loss) in that it is primarily activity-related and potentially reversible through targeted rehabilitation.
Q6 · Definition match · THIS WEEK haemarthrosis (21 Jun · Musculoskeletal)
Which term describes the accumulation of blood within a joint cavity, occurring as a consequence of traumatic injury (e.g., anterior cruciate ligament rupture) or as a complication of anticoagulant therapy?
A) arthralgia
B) synovitis
C) haemarthrosis
D) bursitis
Show Answer
C) haemarthrosis — Haemarthrosis specifically denotes bleeding into the joint space, presenting with rapid joint swelling, warmth, and restricted movement. It is distinguished from a general joint effusion, which may contain synovial fluid, purulent material, or other fluid. ACL tears and tibial plateau fractures are common traumatic causes.
Q7 · Definition match · THIS WEEK plantarflexion (16 Jun · Musculoskeletal)
Which term describes the ankle and foot movement in which the toes point downward away from the body, increasing the angle between the plantar surface of the foot and the posterior leg — as occurs when standing on tiptoe or depressing a car accelerator pedal?
A) dorsiflexion
B) inversion
C) plantarflexion
D) pronation
Show Answer
C) plantarflexion — Plantarflexion decreases the angle at the anterior ankle (i.e., the foot moves away from the shin). The primary plantarflexors are the gastrocnemius and soleus. Normal range is approximately 40–50°. It is the opposite of dorsiflexion, which brings the dorsum of the foot toward the shin.
Q8 · Definition match · REVIEW anaemia (10 Jun · Signs & Symptoms)
Which condition is characterised by a reduction in circulating erythrocytes or haemoglobin concentration, resulting in diminished oxygen-carrying capacity and presenting with fatigue, pallor, dyspnoea on exertion, and tachycardia?
A) polycythaemia
B) thrombocytopaenia
C) leucocytosis
D) anaemia
Show Answer
D) anaemia — Anaemia is defined in adults as haemoglobin < 130 g/L (males) or < 120 g/L (females). Common causes include iron deficiency, chronic disease, vitamin B12 deficiency, and blood loss. In physiotherapy, anaemia is a key precaution for exercise prescription, as it limits aerobic capacity.
Q9 · Collocation · REVIEW synovial (9 Jun · Musculoskeletal)
Complete the clinical collocation: "___ fluid" — the viscous, egg-white–like substance secreted by the inner lining of joint capsules, which lubricates articular cartilage and provides nutrients to avascular structures within the joint.
A) articular
B) interstitial
C) synovial
D) pleural
Show Answer
C) synovial — "Synovial fluid" is the correct and specific term. It is produced by type B synoviocytes in the synovial membrane. Analysis of synovial fluid (viscosity, cell count, crystals) aids in differentiating inflammatory arthritis, infection, and crystal arthropathy. Articular fluid is a non-specific lay term.
Q10 · Collocation · THIS WEEK calcaneal (16 Jun · Musculoskeletal)
Complete the clinical collocation: "calcaneal ___" — referring to the thick fibrous cord that connects the calf muscles to the heel bone, the strongest tendon in the human body and a common site of overuse injury in runners.
A) calcaneal ligament
B) calcaneal tendon
C) calcaneal bursa
D) calcaneal spur
Show Answer
B) calcaneal tendon — The calcaneal tendon (formally tendo calcaneus, commonly called the Achilles tendon) attaches the gastrocnemius and soleus to the posterior calcaneus. "Calcaneal tendon" is the preferred anatomical term in formal clinical documentation. A calcaneal spur and calcaneal bursa are related but distinct structures.

Clinical Case Summary — Physiotherapy Outpatient Department

Mr James Ngata, a 68-year-old retired schoolteacher, was referred to the physiotherapy outpatient department three weeks following right total knee arthroplasty (TKA). His past medical history included well-controlled hypertension managed with amlodipine, and mild chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). On auscultation, bilateral basal crackles were noted, consistent with his known respiratory history.

Initial assessment revealed marked tenderness along the medial joint line of the operative knee, with palpation eliciting a verbal pain score of 6/10. Active range of motion was limited to 0–82° of flexion. Notably, strength testing of the contralateral (left) limb demonstrated relatively preserved quadriceps function at 4+/5, providing a useful functional benchmark for goal-setting and symmetry targets.

The treating physiotherapist identified signs consistent with orthostatic hypotension during the initial standing transfer: Mr Ngata reported dizziness on rising, and blood pressure monitoring confirmed a 26 mmHg drop in systolic pressure. This was attributed to prolonged bed rest and impaired vasodilatation responses in the peripheral vasculature — a recognised consequence of inactivity in older adults with pre-existing vascular comorbidities.

A structured rehabilitation programme was commenced, incorporating progressive weight-bearing, cardiovascular reconditioning, and targeted lower limb strengthening. Short- and long-term goals were established in collaboration with Mr Ngata and his family, with return to independent community ambulation targeted within six weeks.

Q1 · Factual
Why were bilateral basal crackles noted on auscultation?
A) Due to post-operative pneumonia developing after the procedure
B) As a result of a pleural effusion related to heart failure
C) Consistent with his known chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
D) Caused by aspiration of gastric contents during surgery
Show Answer
C) — The passage explicitly states crackles were "consistent with his known respiratory history" of COPD. No other respiratory pathology is mentioned; the other options introduce conditions not described in the text.
Q2 · Factual
What specific measurement confirmed orthostatic hypotension in Mr Ngata?
A) SpO₂ dropped to below 90% on standing
B) Heart rate increased by more than 30 beats per minute
C) Systolic blood pressure fell by 26 mmHg on standing
D) Diastolic blood pressure rose by 15 mmHg on standing
Show Answer
C) — The passage states "blood pressure monitoring confirmed a 26 mmHg drop in systolic pressure." A ≥ 20 mmHg systolic drop on standing meets the clinical definition of orthostatic hypotension.
Q3 · Inferential
Why did the physiotherapist assess quadriceps strength in the contralateral limb?
A) Because the left knee had also been surgically treated
B) To establish a symmetry benchmark and functional goal for rehabilitation
C) To check for signs of deep vein thrombosis in the unaffected leg
D) Because the right limb was too painful to assess at initial consultation
Show Answer
B) — The passage states the contralateral limb's strength provided "a useful functional benchmark for goal-setting and symmetry targets." This is standard practice post-TKA: the unaffected limb guides rehabilitation targets. The other options are not supported by the text.
Q4 · Inferential
What does the reference to impaired vasodilatation responses suggest about the physiological mechanism behind Mr Ngata's dizziness on standing?
A) His antihypertensive medication was causing paradoxical vasospasm
B) His peripheral blood vessels failed to dilate sufficiently to maintain blood pressure on standing
C) Cardiac output had been permanently reduced by the surgical procedure
D) His COPD was limiting oxygen delivery to the peripheral vasculature
Show Answer
B) — Under normal circumstances, peripheral vasodilatation and then reflex vasoconstriction maintain blood pressure on standing. In deconditioned patients, this regulatory response is blunted, leading to venous pooling in the lower limbs and a consequent drop in cerebral perfusion — causing dizziness. The passage attributes this specifically to inactivity, not to medication or cardiac/respiratory causes.
Q5 · Vocabulary in context
In this passage, "tenderness" (paragraph 2) most closely means:
A) Emotional distress reported by the patient about his surgical outcome
B) Pain or discomfort elicited by applying pressure to a body part
C) Generalised inflammation of the joint capsule and surrounding tissues
D) Reduced range of motion due to scar tissue formation
Show Answer
B) — In a clinical context, "tenderness" refers specifically to pain provoked by external palpation or pressure, not spontaneous pain. The passage describes it with the verb "palpation eliciting a verbal pain score of 6/10," confirming that tenderness here means pain on touch. This is a key OET vocabulary distinction: pain = spontaneous; tenderness = pain provoked by examination.

Role Play 1 — Outpatient Physiotherapy Clinic

Setting
Physiotherapy outpatient clinic, Auckland. Three weeks post right ACL reconstruction.
Your role
Physiotherapist (you, the student). The patient card is read by your practice partner.
Patient card (give to partner)
You are Mr David Lee, 45M. You had ACL reconstruction surgery three weeks ago. You are worried because your left (unaffected) knee now feels stiff, and you wonder if something has gone wrong. You want to know how long rehabilitation will take and when you can return to social football.
Your tasks
1. Explain what ACL rehabilitation involves and set realistic expectations.
2. Reassure Mr Lee about his contralateral knee stiffness — explain why this commonly occurs.
3. Explain the importance of strengthening his quadriceps on both sides.
4. Address his question about returning to football.

Suggested opening: "Good morning, Mr Lee. How has your knee been feeling since we last saw you? I also understand you have a concern about your other knee — I'm glad you mentioned it."

"Your rehabilitation programme is designed to..." "The contralateral knee — your left — can stiffen because..." "Strengthening your quadriceps on both sides will help..." "It's common to offload the unaffected limb and..." "We're typically looking at 9 to 12 months before return to..."
Show Model Answer Outline
Model outline:
1. Open: Warm greeting; acknowledge patient's concerns about both knees; confirm he's comfortable.
2. Rehab explanation: Describe the 5 phases of ACL rehabilitation — acute (swelling/pain control), range of motion, strength, neuromuscular control, sport-specific. Explain it typically takes 9–12 months to safely return to cutting/contact sport.
3. Contralateral stiffness: Explain that after injury to one limb, patients instinctively offload it and bear more weight through the other side. This altered gait pattern, combined with reduced overall activity, causes the unaffected limb to stiffen. Reassure him it is not an injury.
4. Quadriceps importance: Explain that quadriceps strength on the surgical side is the key benchmark for return-to-sport clearance (>90% limb symmetry index). Also address bilateral strengthening to prevent compensatory overuse of the contralateral limb.
5. Close: Address football goal positively; set next milestone (e.g., 6-week review). Invite questions.

Role Play 2 — Hospital Inpatient Ward (Post-Cardiac Surgery)

Setting
General surgical ward. Day 2 following coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery.
Your role
Physiotherapist conducting first post-operative mobilisation assessment.
Patient card (give to partner)
You are Mrs Yuki Tanaka, 70F. You had open-heart surgery two days ago. You are anxious about getting up for the first time — you feel unsteady and are worried about falling. You have a long history of high blood pressure. You want to know if it is safe to stand up and what might happen.
Your tasks
1. Explain the risk of orthostatic hypotension in plain language before standing.
2. Relate her hypertension history and bed rest to her current cardiovascular response.
3. Explain vasodilatation in simple terms — why blood vessels need to "open up" and why this may be sluggish.
4. Describe your safety protocol (sit → dangle → stand with monitoring).

Suggested opening: "Good morning, Mrs Tanaka. My name is Leo — I'm one of the physiotherapists. Today we're going to try to get you sitting up and, if you feel ready, standing for the first time. Before we do, I'd like to explain what might happen so you feel prepared."

"Because you have a history of high blood pressure..." "When we stand up, our blood vessels need to open up — we call this vasodilatation..." "Orthostatic hypotension just means a temporary drop in blood pressure..." "If you feel dizzy or lightheaded, please tell me immediately..." "We'll take this very slowly — sit first, then dangle your legs, then stand."
Show Model Answer Outline
Model outline:
1. Open: Introduce yourself; confirm patient's name and how she feels today; acknowledge anxiety with empathy: "It's completely understandable to feel a bit nervous — let's take this at your pace."
2. Rationale for mobilisation: Explain early mobilisation is essential after cardiac surgery to prevent blood clots, chest complications, and muscle loss — reassure her the surgical team has cleared her to begin today.
3. Explain orthostatic hypotension: Use plain language — "When you've been lying flat for two days, sometimes when you first sit or stand, your blood pressure can drop temporarily. This can make you feel dizzy or lightheaded. It's called orthostatic hypotension."
4. Link to vasodilatation and hypertension: Explain simply — "Normally, the blood vessels in your legs open up and then quickly squeeze to keep blood going to your brain. After surgery and bed rest, this reflex can be a bit slow. Because you've had high blood pressure for a long time, the blood vessels may take a little longer to respond."
5. Safety protocol: Describe step-by-step: check BP lying → sit upright (wait 2 min, recheck BP) → dangle legs over edge (wait 2 min) → stand with support (recheck BP). Explain what will trigger sitting back down (dizziness, BP drop >20 mmHg, pallor).
6. Close: Invite questions; reassure; "You're doing wonderfully — let's take it one step at a time."
