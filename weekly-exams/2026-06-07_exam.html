Fill in the Blank (Questions 1–5)
Question 1 · Fill in the Blank THIS WEEK · glenohumeral
The physiotherapist assessed the patient for _______ instability following a traumatic shoulder dislocation sustained during a rugby tackle, noting apprehension on external rotation at 90° of abduction.
A) patellofemoral
B) acromioclavicular
C) glenohumeral
D) sacroiliac
Show Answer
C) glenohumeral. The glenohumeral joint is the ball-and-socket joint of the shoulder. Instability here refers to excessive or symptomatic translation of the humeral head within the glenoid fossa, commonly following traumatic dislocation. The other options refer to different joints: patellofemoral (knee cap), acromioclavicular (shoulder tip), and sacroiliac (pelvis).
Question 2 · Fill in the Blank THIS WEEK · hypoxaemia
The post-operative patient developed _______ overnight, with SpO₂ dropping to 88% on room air, necessitating the commencement of supplemental oxygen via nasal cannulae.
A) hypertension
B) hypoxaemia
C) hypercapnia
D) haemostasis
Show Answer
B) hypoxaemia. Hypoxaemia is an abnormally low level of oxygen in the blood, reflected by a reduced SpO₂. It is a common post-operative complication managed with supplemental oxygen. Hypercapnia (option C) refers to elevated carbon dioxide — a related but distinct condition. Haemostasis (D) is the arrest of bleeding and is unrelated.
Question 3 · Fill in the Blank THIS WEEK · vestibular
Following a mild traumatic brain injury, the patient was referred for _______ rehabilitation to address persistent dizziness, nystagmus, and difficulty maintaining postural stability during functional tasks.
A) cardiac
B) pulmonary
C) vestibular
D) cognitive
Show Answer
C) vestibular. The vestibular system, located in the inner ear, regulates balance and spatial orientation. Vestibular rehabilitation is a specialist physiotherapy programme targeting dizziness, nystagmus, and postural instability. The symptoms described — particularly nystagmus and dizziness post-head injury — are hallmark vestibular presentations.
Question 4 · Fill in the Blank PREVIOUS · lymphoedema
The patient presented with unilateral left upper limb _______ six months following axillary lymph node dissection for breast cancer, requiring fitting for a graduated compression garment.
A) oedema
B) lymphoedema
C) induration
D) ecchymosis
Show Answer
B) lymphoedema. Lymphoedema is chronic swelling caused by impaired lymphatic drainage, commonly developing after lymph node removal. It is distinct from general oedema (A), which has broader causes. Induration (C) refers to abnormal tissue hardening; ecchymosis (D) is bruising — neither fits the clinical picture described.
Question 5 · Fill in the Blank PREVIOUS · nociceptive
The treating physiotherapist differentiated _______ pain, arising from ongoing peripheral tissue damage, from central sensitisation as the primary mechanism driving the patient's chronic low back pain presentation.
A) neuropathic
B) discogenic
C) nociceptive
D) visceral
Show Answer
C) nociceptive. Nociceptive pain results from activation of nociceptors in response to actual or threatened tissue damage. It is one of the three primary pain mechanisms assessed in physiotherapy (alongside neuropathic and nociplastic/central sensitisation). Discogenic (B) refers specifically to disc-related pain — a subtype, not a broad mechanism.
Definition Match (Questions 6–8)
Question 6 · Definition Match THIS WEEK · kinesiophobia
Which of the following terms describes an excessive, irrational fear of movement or physical activity, typically arising after injury, that leads to avoidance behaviour and perpetuates functional disability?
A) nociception
B) perseveration
C) kinesiophobia
D) monoplegia
Show Answer
C) kinesiophobia. From the Greek kinesis (movement) + phobia (fear). It is measured clinically using the Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK). Scores above 37 indicate high kinesiophobia and are associated with poorer rehabilitation outcomes. Perseveration (B) is repetitive behaviour seen in neurological conditions; monoplegia (D) is paralysis of one limb.
Question 7 · Definition Match THIS WEEK · arthroscopy
Which term refers to a minimally invasive surgical procedure in which a fibre-optic camera (arthroscope) is inserted into a joint to directly visualise, diagnose, and treat internal structures such as the meniscus or articular cartilage?
A) electromyography
B) arthroscopy
C) bronchoscopy
D) acupuncture
Show Answer
B) arthroscopy. Arthroscopy (from Greek arthron = joint, skopein = to look) is widely used in physiotherapy practice for post-operative rehabilitation contexts, including knee (meniscal repair, ACL reconstruction) and shoulder procedures. Bronchoscopy (C) is visualisation of the airways — same technique, different anatomical site.
Question 8 · Definition Match PREVIOUS · sciatica
Which of the following describes radiating pain that travels along the distribution of the sciatic nerve, typically originating in the lumbar spine and extending through the buttock and down the posterior or lateral aspect of the leg?
A) neuralgia
B) monoplegia
C) sciatica
D) fibromyalgia
Show Answer
C) sciatica. Sciatica is caused by irritation or compression of the sciatic nerve, most commonly due to lumbar disc herniation at L4/5 or L5/S1. The distribution of symptoms — buttock to posterior leg — is the key differentiator. Neuralgia (A) is a broader term for nerve pain; fibromyalgia (D) is a central sensitisation syndrome with widespread pain.
Collocation Complete (Questions 9–10)
Question 9 · Collocation THIS WEEK · fasciitis
A 44-year-old recreational runner presented with inferior heel pain, worst with the first steps in the morning. Examination revealed point tenderness at the calcaneal insertion. The physiotherapist documented a diagnosis of _______ fasciitis.
A) plantar
B) iliotibial
C) calcaneal
D) tibial
Show Answer
A) plantar. "Plantar fasciitis" is the correct clinical collocation — inflammation of the plantar fascia at its calcaneal origin. It is the most common cause of heel pain in active adults. "Plantar" describes the sole of the foot. The other options do not form standard clinical collocations with "fasciitis" in this context.
Question 10 · Collocation PREVIOUS · epicondylitis
A 38-year-old office worker presented with pain over the outer aspect of the elbow, aggravated by gripping and wrist extension. Resisted wrist extension reproduced symptoms. The physiotherapist recorded _______ epicondylitis as the working diagnosis.
A) lateral
B) medial
C) distal
D) proximal
Show Answer
A) lateral. "Lateral epicondylitis" (colloquially known as tennis elbow) affects the common extensor origin at the lateral humeral epicondyle. The classic presentation is pain on the outer elbow worsened by gripping and resisted wrist/finger extension. Medial epicondylitis (B) — golfer's elbow — involves the flexor-pronator origin and causes medial elbow pain.

Kinesiophobia and Its Influence on Recovery Following Glenohumeral Instability Repair

Patients undergoing surgical stabilisation for recurrent glenohumeral instability frequently face significant psychological barriers to recovery. A prospective cohort study conducted across three New Zealand orthopaedic units evaluated 54 patients at six and twelve weeks following Bankart repair. Assessors screened participants for kinesiophobia using the Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK) at the initial post-operative physiotherapy appointment.


Of the cohort, 58% scored above 37 on the TSK, indicating clinically significant kinesiophobia. These patients reported paroxysmal episodes of sharp, catching pain during even guided passive range-of-motion exercises, which reinforced their avoidance of movement. Physiotherapists noted that patients with high TSK scores demonstrated measurably reduced shoulder inflammation resolution rates at six weeks, attributed in part to reduced participation in hydrotherapy and active rehabilitation.


Two participants experienced transient desaturation during early aquatic sessions, likely secondary to breath-holding associated with pain anticipation; both resolved with coaching around breathing technique. A further complication arose in three patients who developed mild hypoxaemia during anaesthetic recovery but were discharged without ongoing respiratory concerns.


The study concluded that physiotherapists should routinely screen for kinesiophobia post-operatively and integrate graded exposure therapy into the rehabilitation pathway. Addressing the cognitive and emotional dimensions of recovery was found to be as clinically important as optimising biomechanical outcomes.

Reading Question 1 · Factual
Which assessment tool was used to screen participants for kinesiophobia in the study?
A) Visual Analogue Scale (VAS)
B) Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK)
C) DASH Questionnaire
D) Oswestry Disability Index (ODI)
Show Answer
B) Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK). The passage explicitly states: "Assessors screened participants for kinesiophobia using the Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK)." The VAS measures pain intensity, the DASH measures upper limb function, and the ODI measures low back pain disability — none are kinesiophobia-specific tools.
Reading Question 2 · Factual
What percentage of participants demonstrated clinically significant kinesiophobia at their initial post-operative physiotherapy appointment?
A) 37%
B) 54%
C) 58%
D) 62%
Show Answer
C) 58%. The passage states "58% scored above 37 on the TSK, indicating clinically significant kinesiophobia." Note: 54 is the total number of patients in the cohort, and 37 is the TSK cut-off score — these are distractors drawn directly from the text.
Reading Question 3 · Inferential
The passage implies that reduced inflammation resolution at six weeks in high-TSK patients was primarily due to:
A) surgical complications from the Bankart repair
B) inadequate post-operative analgesia
C) reduced engagement in active rehabilitation and hydrotherapy
D) respiratory complications during recovery
Show Answer
C) reduced engagement in active rehabilitation and hydrotherapy. The passage states inflammation resolution was reduced "attributed in part to reduced participation in hydrotherapy and active rehabilitation" — a direct consequence of kinesiophobia-driven avoidance. The respiratory complications (D) affected a different and smaller subgroup and were not linked to inflammation.
Reading Question 4 · Inferential
Why did two participants experience transient desaturation during aquatic sessions?
A) They had undiagnosed asthma exacerbated by pool chlorine
B) They held their breath due to anticipatory pain anxiety
C) They had residual hypoxaemia from the anaesthetic
D) They exercised at an intensity exceeding their cardiorespiratory capacity
Show Answer
B) they held their breath due to anticipatory pain anxiety. The passage specifies the cause as "breath-holding associated with pain anticipation" — a behavioural consequence of kinesiophobia during exercise. Both cases resolved with breathing coaching, further supporting a behavioural rather than pathological cause.
Reading Question 5 · Vocabulary in Context
In the second paragraph, the word paroxysmal most closely means:
A) persistent and constant throughout the day
B) sudden in onset, intense, and intermittent
C) gradual and progressive over several weeks
D) deep, aching, and difficult to localise
Show Answer
B) sudden in onset, intense, and intermittent. Paroxysmal (from Greek paroxysmos = sudden fit) describes events that occur abruptly and episodically — the opposite of constant. In the passage, the "catching" quality and occurrence "during even guided passive range-of-motion" supports this — unexpected, sharp, episodic pain rather than a continuous background ache.

OET Speaking — Role Plays

⏱ 5 min each

Role Play 1 — Addressing Kinesiophobia After Knee Reconstruction

Setting: Outpatient physiotherapy clinic, Auckland. You are the treating physiotherapist.

Patient: Ms Sarah Park, 34-year-old secondary school teacher. She is 14 weeks post-ACL reconstruction and has stalled significantly in her rehabilitation. She scored 45 on the TSK. She is reluctant to perform landing and jumping tasks, stating: "Every time I try to jump or run, I'm terrified I'll snap it again."

Your task:

  • Validate Ms Park's concerns about re-injury in an empathetic, non-dismissive way
  • Explain what kinesiophobia is and how it affects her recovery
  • Introduce the concept of graded exposure therapy and reassure her about the process
  • Avoid escalating her anxiety — use reassuring, patient-centred language

Vocabulary to use: THIS WEEK kinesiophobia, validate, escalate

Suggested opening: "Ms Park, I can hear how anxious you've been feeling about your knee, and I want to take some time today to really address what's going on — because I think what you're experiencing actually has a name, and it's something we can work through together."

"What you're feeling is completely understandable..." "This fear of movement is actually called kinesiophobia..." "We want to validate your concerns, not dismiss them..." "We'll progress gradually so we don't escalate your anxiety..." "The evidence shows that graded exposure is very effective..."
Show Model Answer Outline
1. Acknowledge and validate (1 min): Open by normalising her fear. Acknowledge that ACL re-rupture is a real risk that many patients think about, and that her feelings make sense after a serious injury. Use "I want to validate what you're feeling — this is a very common experience after reconstructive surgery."

2. Explain kinesiophobia (1 min): Describe it as a recognised pattern where fear of pain or re-injury leads to movement avoidance, which paradoxically slows healing and weakens the joint. Use simple, non-threatening language: "The brain can become overly protective after an injury — it sends strong pain or fear signals even when the knee is structurally healed and safe to load."

3. Introduce graded exposure (2 min): Explain that you will start with low-threat movements she already finds manageable, then very gradually introduce higher-load tasks, only progressing when she feels ready. Emphasise that you will never escalate the programme faster than her confidence allows.

4. Set a collaborative goal (1 min): Ask her what activity she most wants to return to. Build motivation around her personal goal (e.g. returning to recreational sport, keeping up with students). Finish with a clear plan for today's session — starting with something well within her comfort zone.

Role Play 2 — Post-Arthroscopy Rehabilitation Education

Setting: Orthopaedic outpatient physiotherapy, Middlemore Hospital. You are the physiotherapist conducting the first post-operative consultation.

Patient: Mr James Watkins, 26-year-old electrician. He is 8 days post right knee arthroscopy for a medial meniscal repair. He is anxious about the swelling around his knee and frustrated about the slow recovery. He says: "The surgeon said the op went fine, but my knee is still really swollen and hot — is that normal? When can I go back to work and start running again?"

Your task:

  • Explain what the arthroscopy involved and reassure him that residual inflammation is expected
  • Address his concerns about the swelling and heat in the joint
  • Outline a realistic rehabilitation timeline, including return-to-work and return-to-running milestones
  • Discuss the importance of avoiding joint instability during early rehabilitation

Vocabulary to use: THIS WEEK arthroscopy, inflammation, instability

Suggested opening: "Mr Watkins, thank you for coming in. It sounds like you have some really important questions — let me start by explaining exactly what happened during the arthroscopy and why you're experiencing those symptoms right now."

"The arthroscopy involved small incisions called portals..." "Some degree of inflammation is expected and normal at this stage..." "The swelling and warmth are signs that healing is taking place..." "We need to avoid any activities that create joint instability..." "Return to running is typically around 12 weeks for meniscal repair..."
Show Model Answer Outline
1. Explain the arthroscopy (1 min): Briefly describe what arthroscopy involved — small portals, a camera inserted into the joint, and the meniscal repair technique (sutures or anchors). Emphasise it was minimally invasive but still involves healing tissues. "Although the arthroscopy is keyhole surgery, the repair inside the joint still requires significant healing time — the meniscus has poor blood supply and needs protecting."

2. Normalise the inflammation (1 min): Explain that warmth, swelling, and stiffness at 8 days are entirely expected signs of the body's healing response. Inflammation is the first phase of tissue repair. Reassure him the joint is not infected. Advise continuing RICE (rest, ice, compression, elevation) and elevating at night.

3. Address instability risk (1 min): Explain why avoiding loaded pivoting, twisting, and deep squatting is critical during early recovery — the repaired meniscus cannot yet withstand forces that create joint instability, which could re-tear the repair. "If you were to return to work too soon doing ladder work or kneeling, the forces on the joint could create instability and damage the repair."

4. Outline timeline (2 min): Provide realistic milestones — light walking: now; desk work: 2 weeks; light duties (no kneeling or squatting): 4–6 weeks; full manual work: 10–12 weeks; return to running: 12 weeks; sport: 4–6 months. Emphasise these are approximate and depend on his progress in physiotherapy.
