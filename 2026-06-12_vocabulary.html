Medical Definition
The use of roundabout or indirect language to express an idea; in clinical practice, a compensatory communication strategy employed by patients with aphasia who cannot retrieve the intended word, in which they describe the item's function or properties instead.
繁體中文

OET Clinical Example

Related Words
aphasia (language disorder) anomia (word-finding difficulty) paraphasia (word substitution error) word-finding difficulty compensatory strategy
Word 4 of 5
paraphasia
/ˌpærəˈfeɪzɪə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A type of speech error characteristic of aphasia in which the patient substitutes an incorrect word or sound for the intended one; classified as verbal (whole-word substitution), phonemic (sound substitution), or semantic (substitution of a related word).
繁體中文
換語症；語言替換症
Common Collocations
verbal paraphasia phonemic paraphasia semantic paraphasia neologistic paraphasia frequent paraphasia
OET Clinical Example
"Formal speech assessment identified frequent semantic paraphasia, with the patient substituting semantically related words — for example, saying 'spoon' when intending to say 'fork' — consistent with posterior temporal lobe involvement."
Related Words
aphasia (broader language disorder) circumlocution (compensatory description) anomia (inability to name objects) dysphasia (milder language impairment) jargon aphasia (severe form)

✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 52)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr. David Tran, a 54-year-old male with a recent diagnosis of relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis, was referred to the physiotherapy department by his neurologist for rehabilitation following a recent acute relapse affecting his brainstem and cerebellum.

On neurological examination, he demonstrated internuclear ophthalmoplegia with failure of adduction in the right eye on leftward gaze, as well as bilateral upper limb intention tremor. Sensory assessment revealed cutaneous hyperaesthesia over the right forearm and hand, with light touch reported as burning and uncomfortable, consistent with central sensitisation.

Formal speech and language review identified intermittent word-finding difficulties. The patient frequently employed circumlocution to compensate, and occasional instances of semantic paraphasia were noted — for example, substituting 'window' for 'door' during naming tasks. He was, however, able to follow complex two-step instructions without difficulty.

During the initial assessment, the physiotherapist used clear signposting to guide Mr. Tran through each stage of the consultation, explicitly orienting him before moving between the physical examination, discussion of findings, and goal-setting components of the session. This approach was well received and appeared to reduce the patient's visible anxiety.

Goals of physiotherapy include upper limb coordination training, balance and gait rehabilitation, fatigue management education, and sensory desensitisation strategies for the affected upper limb.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When writing referral letters for neurological patients, name the specific type of deficit (e.g. "internuclear ophthalmoplegia" not just "eye movement problems"; "semantic paraphasia" not just "speech difficulties"). This level of precision demonstrates clinical proficiency and provides the receiving clinician with actionable information.
