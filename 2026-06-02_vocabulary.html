perseveration
/pəˌsev.əˈreɪ.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
The uncontrollable, involuntary repetition of a word, phrase, gesture, or action beyond the point at which it is appropriate; a neurological sign commonly observed following stroke, traumatic brain injury, or in dementia, reflecting impaired inhibitory control in the frontal lobes.
繁體中文
持續重複症（固執症）— 無法停止重複某一言語、動作或思維，見於腦損傷或失智症
Common Collocations
verbal perseveration motor perseveration marked perseveration perseveration of speech demonstrate perseveration
OET Clinical Example
"On cognitive assessment, Mr. Tran demonstrated marked verbal perseveration, repeatedly returning to topics already addressed and unable to redirect his responses, consistent with his documented left frontal lobe lesion following ischaemic stroke."
Related Words
perseverate (verb) perseverative (adj) confabulation (false memory filling) aphasia (language impairment) perseverance (antonym — positive continued effort)
Word 2 of 5
glenohumeral
/ˌɡliː.nəʊˈhjuː.mər.əl/ adjective 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal
Medical Definition
Relating to the glenoid fossa of the scapula and the head of the humerus; pertaining to the true shoulder joint, which is a ball-and-socket synovial joint providing the greatest range of motion of any joint in the body.
繁體中文
盂肱的（肩關節的）— 指肩胛骨關節盂與肱骨頭所構成的肩關節
Common Collocations
glenohumeral joint glenohumeral instability glenohumeral range of motion glenohumeral ligament glenohumeral arthritis
OET Clinical Example
"Assessment revealed restricted glenohumeral external rotation of 30° on the right compared with 80° on the left, with a painful arc between 60° and 120° of abduction, findings consistent with rotator cuff impingement at the glenohumeral joint."
Related Words
glenoid (adj/noun — the socket) humeral (adj — relating to humerus) scapulohumeral (broader shoulder complex) acromioclavicular (AC joint) capsulitis (adhesive capsulitis — frozen shoulder)
Word 3 of 5
kinesiophobia
/kɪˌniː.zi.əˈfəʊ.bi.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
Patient Communication 神經 Neurological
Medical Definition
An excessive, irrational fear of movement or physical activity, driven by the belief that movement will cause re-injury or worsening of pain; commonly assessed using the Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia and strongly associated with chronic pain, fear-avoidance behaviour, and poor rehabilitation outcomes.
繁體中文
運動恐懼症 — 對運動或活動的過度恐懼，害怕動作會導致再受傷，常見於慢性疼痛患者
Common Collocations
significant kinesiophobia kinesiophobia screening address kinesiophobia Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia kinesiophobia score
OET Clinical Example
"Mrs. Patel scored 44 out of 68 on the Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia, indicating high levels of movement-related fear; graded exposure therapy and pain neuroscience education have been incorporated into her physiotherapy programme to address her kinesiophobia and reduce avoidance behaviour."
Related Words
fear-avoidance model (theoretical framework) Tampa Scale (TSK — assessment tool) catastrophising (cognitive pattern) pain neuroscience education / PNE graded exposure (treatment approach)
Word 4 of 5
monoplegia
/ˌmɒn.əˈpliː.dʒi.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Complete paralysis or total loss of voluntary movement affecting a single limb; may result from cortical lesions, peripheral nerve injury, or brachial plexus damage, and is distinguished from hemiplegia by its isolated involvement of one extremity.
繁體中文
單肢癱瘓 — 單一肢體（一隻手或一隻腳）完全喪失自主運動能力
Common Collocations
right upper limb monoplegia monoplegia secondary to stroke complete monoplegia monoplegia following TBI flaccid monoplegia
OET Clinical Example
"The patient was admitted following a haemorrhagic stroke affecting the right motor cortex, presenting with right upper limb monoplegia and intact strength in all remaining extremities; physiotherapy assessment confirmed complete absence of voluntary motor function in the affected arm, with MRC grade 0 throughout."
Related Words
hemiplegia (one-sided paralysis) paraplegia (both lower limbs) tetraplegia (all four limbs) diplegia (bilateral same-region paralysis) -plegia suffix (paralysis)
Word 5 of 5
osteomalacia
/ˌɒs.ti.əʊ.məˈleɪ.ʃ.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Softening of the bones in adults due to defective mineralisation of newly formed osteoid, most commonly caused by vitamin D deficiency or malabsorption; presents with diffuse bone pain, proximal muscle weakness, and an increased risk of fragility fractures. The paediatric equivalent is rickets.
繁體中文
骨軟化症 — 成人因維生素D缺乏致骨骼礦化不足，導致骨骼軟化、骨痛及近端肌無力
Common Collocations
vitamin D-related osteomalacia present with osteomalacia confirmed osteomalacia osteomalacia and fracture risk osteomalacia secondary to malabsorption
OET Clinical Example
"Biochemical investigations confirmed osteomalacia with serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D of 12 nmol/L; the patient was commenced on high-dose vitamin D supplementation and calcium, and physiotherapy was recommended to address the associated proximal muscle weakness, balance deficits, and elevated falls risk."
Related Words
rickets (paediatric equivalent) osteoporosis (reduced bone density — different mechanism) osteoid (unmineralised bone matrix) mineralisation (bone hardening process) osteomalacic (adj)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 42)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr. James Wu, a 62-year-old male with a background of ischaemic stroke (three months prior) and recently confirmed vitamin D deficiency, was referred to the outpatient physiotherapy department for comprehensive rehabilitation.

Neuropsychological assessment identified marked verbal perseveration during standardised cognitive testing, with Mr. Wu unable to shift topic or task despite repeated redirection. This is consistent with his documented left frontal lobe infarct and has significant implications for the delivery of patient education and rehabilitation instructions.

On musculoskeletal assessment, he demonstrated restricted glenohumeral range of motion on the affected right side, with active abduction limited to 45° and external rotation to 20°, attributable to prolonged disuse and capsular tightening following his neurological event. Additionally, biochemical results confirmed osteomalacia (serum vitamin D 14 nmol/L), and he reported diffuse lower limb bone pain and proximal muscle weakness contributing to balance impairment.

Screening using the Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia revealed a score of 46, indicating significant kinesiophobia; Mr. Wu expressed reluctance to bear weight through his right lower limb for fear of falling. He currently presents with right upper limb monoplegia, with MRC grade 0 throughout the right arm; all other extremities demonstrate antigravity strength.

Physiotherapy goals include graded exposure to address kinesiophobia, glenohumeral mobilisation, proximal strengthening to counter the effects of osteomalacia, falls prevention education, and strategies to support communication given perseveration.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting neurological and musculoskeletal findings together, use precise anatomical descriptors (e.g. "glenohumeral range of motion"), quantify deficits where possible (e.g. "MRC grade 0", "TSK score 46"), and link psychosocial factors (e.g. kinesiophobia) directly to functional outcomes. This demonstrates the clinical reasoning examiners expect in OET referral letters.
