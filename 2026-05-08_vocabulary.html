valgus
/ˈvæl.ɡəs/ adjective 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Describing a deformity in which the distal part of a limb or body segment is angled outward (away from the midline) relative to the proximal joint; for example, a lateral deviation of the knee or great toe.

Related Words

myofascial
/ˌmaɪ.əʊˈfæʃ.əl/ adjective 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Relating to or involving both the muscle tissue and the surrounding connective tissue (fascia); most commonly encountered in the context of myofascial pain syndrome, where trigger points within the muscle and fascia produce localised or referred pain.
繁體中文
肌筋膜的
Common Collocations
myofascial pain myofascial trigger point myofascial release myofascial tension myofascial syndrome
OET Clinical Example
"The patient presents with chronic myofascial pain affecting the upper trapezius and levator scapulae bilaterally, with multiple active trigger points eliciting referred pain to the occiput on palpation."
Related Words
fascia (connective tissue) trigger point myofascial release (therapy) myofascial pain syndrome (MPS) referred pain

allodynia
/ˌæl.əʊˈdɪn.i.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
Signs & Symptoms 神經 Neurological
Medical Definition
Pain elicited by a stimulus that does not normally provoke pain, such as light touch, gentle pressure, or temperature change; a hallmark feature of central sensitisation and neuropathic pain conditions.
繁體中文
觸痛（異位痛）；正常無痛刺激引發的疼痛
Common Collocations
mechanical allodynia tactile allodynia thermal allodynia allodynia to light touch allodynia in the affected area
OET Clinical Example
"The patient demonstrated marked mechanical allodynia over the anterior thigh and medial knee, with light touch consistently triggering pain rated 7/10 on the Numeric Rating Scale, consistent with a presentation of central sensitisation."
Related Words
hyperalgesia (exaggerated pain to painful stimulus) central sensitisation neuropathic pain allodynic (adjective) pain sensitisation

✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 19)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr David Park, a 34-year-old male tradesman, was referred to your physiotherapy department following a right knee injury sustained during a football match three weeks ago. MRI confirmed a grade II medial collateral ligament sprain with associated joint effusion.

On initial assessment, Mr Park presented with a resting posture demonstrating mild dynamic knee valgus during single-leg stance on the right, indicative of gluteal weakness and altered lower limb biomechanics. He reported moderate pain (6/10 NRS) on weight-bearing activities, with notable guarding of the right quadriceps and hamstrings on passive range of motion testing, limiting knee flexion to 85 degrees.

Palpation of the medial joint line revealed localised erythema and warmth consistent with the subacute inflammatory phase of healing. The patient also reported allodynia to light touch over the proximal medial tibia, suggesting a degree of peripheral sensitisation that will need to be considered in pain management planning.

In addition, palpation of the right iliotibial band and gluteus medius identified significant myofascial trigger points producing referred pain to the lateral knee. Treatment will incorporate progressive neuromuscular rehabilitation, trigger point therapy, and a graduated return-to-sport programme.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting musculoskeletal findings, specify the degree of deformity or restriction (e.g. "85 degrees of flexion"), the nature of signs (e.g. "erythema and warmth"), and any neurological overlay (e.g. allodynia suggesting sensitisation). This level of clinical detail is expected in OET Writing Task referral letters.
