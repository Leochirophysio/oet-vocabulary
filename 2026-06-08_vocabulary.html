alveolar
/ælˈviː.ə.lər/ adjective 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary
Medical Definition
Relating to the alveoli — the tiny air sacs at the end of the bronchioles in the lungs where gas exchange (oxygen and carbon dioxide) takes place between the air and the bloodstream.

Medical Definition
Abnormal enlargement of the heart, typically identified on chest X-ray or echocardiogram. It may result from hypertension, cardiomyopathy, valve disease, or chronic volume overload.

Common Collocations

Related Words

Word 3 of 5
pleurodesis
/plʊəˈrɒd.ɪ.sɪs/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
Procedures & Interventions 心肺 Cardiopulmonary
Medical Definition
A procedure that creates artificial adhesion between the two layers of the pleura (visceral and parietal), obliterating the pleural space to prevent recurrent pleural effusion or pneumothorax. Commonly performed using talc or chemical irritants.
繁體中文
胸膜固定術
Common Collocations
talc pleurodesis chemical pleurodesis surgical pleurodesis undergo pleurodesis pleurodesis for recurrent effusion
OET Clinical Example
"Following his third recurrence of malignant pleural effusion, Mr Kim underwent talc pleurodesis via video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery; physiotherapy input is requested to support post-operative breathing exercises and early mobilisation."
Related Words
pleura (noun — membrane lining) pleural (adj) pleurisy (pleural inflammation — Day 15) thoracocentesis (fluid drainage — Day 13) -desis (suffix: surgical fixation)

Word 5 of 5
hyperventilation
/ˌhaɪ.pə.ˌven.tɪˈleɪ.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Abnormally rapid or deep breathing that exceeds the body's metabolic needs, causing a reduction in arterial carbon dioxide (hypocapnia). It frequently presents with perioral tingling, lightheadedness, and chest tightness, and may be triggered by anxiety, pain, or metabolic disturbance.
繁體中文
過度換氣；換氣過度
Common Collocations
acute hyperventilation hyperventilation syndrome anxiety-induced hyperventilation hyperventilation during exercise trigger hyperventilation
OET Clinical Example
"The patient presented with an episode of acute hyperventilation secondary to pain and anxiety post-operatively; breathing retraining using diaphragmatic techniques was initiated and the patient responded well within ten minutes."
Related Words
hyperventilate (verb) hypoventilation (antonym — too little breathing) hypocapnia (low CO₂ in blood) tachypnoea (fast rate — Day 6) breathing retraining (physio intervention)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 48)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr David Chan, a 67-year-old male with a background of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and recently diagnosed cardiomegaly secondary to longstanding hypertension, was admitted to the respiratory ward following an acute exacerbation.

During admission, he experienced an episode of hyperventilation precipitated by procedure-related anxiety, presenting with perioral tingling and lightheadedness. Breathing retraining using diaphragmatic and pursed-lip techniques was initiated by the physiotherapy team with satisfactory effect.

His chest X-ray on admission demonstrated bilateral alveolar infiltrates in the lower zones alongside the cardiomegaly, for which echocardiography has been arranged. He also underwent elective talc pleurodesis six weeks prior for recurrent malignant pleural effusion and reports mild residual chest wall discomfort at the procedure site. An indwelling urinary catheterisation is currently in situ pending urological review.

Mr Chan is referred to physiotherapy for pulmonary rehabilitation, ongoing breathing retraining, and progressive mobilisation as tolerated, with due consideration of his multiple comorbidities and post-pleurodesis chest wall sensitivity.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting procedures in referral letters, specify the type (e.g. "talc pleurodesis", "indwelling catheterisation"), the timing (e.g. "six weeks prior"), and any ongoing sequelae (e.g. "residual chest wall discomfort"). This gives the receiving clinician a complete picture without requiring them to access previous notes.
