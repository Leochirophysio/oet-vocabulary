spondylosis
/ˌspɒn.dɪˈləʊ.sɪs/ noun (uncountable) 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Degenerative disease of the spine involving the breakdown of intervertebral discs and the formation of osteophytes (bone spurs), most commonly affecting the cervical or lumbar regions. Associated with ageing and repetitive mechanical stress.

OET Clinical Example

Related Words
spondylitis (inflammatory) spondylolisthesis (slippage) spondylolysis (stress fracture) osteophyte (bone spur) disc degeneration

avulsion
/əˈvʌl.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
The forcible tearing away of a body structure from its attachment, most commonly a fragment of bone pulled off at the site of a ligament or tendon insertion. Occurs due to sudden, high-force muscular contraction or traction injury.
繁體中文

Related Words

dorsiflexion
/ˌdɔː.sɪˈflek.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal
Medical Definition
Movement of the foot upwards towards the shin, reducing the angle between the dorsum of the foot and the anterior aspect of the leg. Also refers to backward bending of the hand at the wrist. Essential for normal gait and stair climbing.
繁體中文
背屈（足部向上彎曲）
Common Collocations

OET Clinical Example
"Assessment reveals restricted right ankle dorsiflexion of 5 degrees (compared with 15 degrees on the left), which is significantly limiting Mr Park's ability to ascend stairs and perform a full squat during functional tasks."
Related Words
dorsiflexor (noun — muscle) plantarflexion (antonym) plantarflex (verb) inversion / eversion range of motion / ROM
Word 5 of 5
myelopathy
/ˌmaɪ.ɪˈlɒp.ə.θi/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Dysfunction or pathological change of the spinal cord, resulting in motor and sensory deficits. Commonly caused by cord compression from degenerative spondylosis, herniated disc, or canal stenosis. Presents with limb weakness, gait disturbance, and upper motor neurone signs.
繁體中文
脊髓病變
Common Collocations
cervical myelopathy compressive myelopathy spondylotic myelopathy progressive myelopathy signs of myelopathy
OET Clinical Example
"Mr Patel has been referred for physiotherapy management of cervical myelopathy secondary to spondylosis at C4–C5, presenting with bilateral hand clumsiness, gait instability, and a positive Lhermitte's sign on cervical flexion."
Related Words
myelopathic (adj) radiculopathy (nerve root) neuropathy (broader term) myelitis (inflammatory) stenosis (cause)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 26)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr David Lim, a 62-year-old retired builder, was referred to your physiotherapy outpatient department by his neurosurgeon following a diagnosis of cervical spondylosis with early myelopathy at C4–C5 and C5–C6.

He reports an exacerbation of his neck pain and bilateral upper limb heaviness over the past three weeks, triggered by a period of sustained overhead work in the garden. Neurological examination reveals diminished grip strength bilaterally, a positive Hoffman's sign, and impaired proprioception in both hands.

Gait assessment demonstrates a broad-based, unsteady pattern, and the patient confirms he has begun using a walking stick for community ambulation. Ankle range of motion screening reveals bilateral dorsiflexion restriction to 8 degrees, which is contributing to his gait instability. Imaging has also identified a small avulsion fracture at the right medial epicondyle, sustained during a recent fall.

Physiotherapy goals include cervical stabilisation exercises, upper limb neurological rehabilitation, gait retraining, and ankle dorsiflexion mobility work. The patient has been counselled regarding red flag symptoms necessitating urgent review.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting spinal pathology, specify the vertebral level (e.g. "C4–C5"), the nature of cord involvement (e.g. "myelopathy" vs "radiculopathy"), and the functional impact on mobility. Avoid generic terms like "neck problem" — precise anatomical language demonstrates clinical credibility in OET Writing tasks.
