dysarthria
/dɪsˈɑː.θri.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A motor speech disorder caused by weakness, incoordination, or paralysis of the muscles used for speaking, resulting in slurred, slow, or otherwise unclear speech. It arises from neurological damage rather than a language-processing deficit.
繁體中文
構音障礙
Common Collocations
spastic dysarthria severe dysarthria dysarthria following stroke dysarthric speech mild dysarthria
OET Clinical Example
"The patient presented with marked dysarthria following a left-hemisphere CVA, rendering speech difficult to understand; referral to speech-language therapy has been arranged as part of the multidisciplinary rehabilitation programme."
Related Words
dysarthric (adj) aphasia (language loss) dysphasia (language impairment) anarthria (severe — no speech) slurred speech (lay term)

aphasia
/əˈfeɪ.zi.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Complete or partial loss of the ability to communicate through language — including speaking, understanding, reading, and writing — resulting from damage to language centres of the brain, most commonly following stroke.
繁體中文
失語症
Common Collocations
expressive aphasia receptive aphasia global aphasia Broca's aphasia aphasia following stroke

Related Words

ataxia
/əˈtæk.si.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Loss of full control of bodily movements due to impaired muscle coordination; typically results from dysfunction of the cerebellum or sensory pathways, and presents as unsteady gait, poor balance, and incoordination of limb movements.
繁體中文
共濟失調
Common Collocations

OET Clinical Example
"The patient demonstrated significant cerebellar ataxia on examination, with a wide-based gait, positive Romberg test, and marked dysmetria on finger-nose testing bilaterally; neurological review has been requested."
Related Words
ataxic (adj) dysmetria (overshooting movements) dysdiadochokinesia (impaired rapid alternating) Romberg test (clinical assessment) incoordination (lay term)
Word 4 of 5
lordosis
/lɔːˈdəʊ.sɪs/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal
Medical Definition
An excessive inward (anterior) curvature of the lumbar or cervical spine. Mild lordosis is a normal feature of spinal alignment; however, excessive or pathological lordosis is associated with lower back pain, anterior pelvic tilt, and altered movement mechanics.
繁體中文
脊椎前凸（前彎過大）
Common Collocations
lumbar lordosis cervical lordosis excessive lordosis loss of lumbar lordosis increased lordosis
OET Clinical Example
"Postural assessment revealed marked lumbar lordosis with associated anterior pelvic tilt and shortened hip flexors, which was identified as a contributing factor to the patient's chronic non-specific lower back pain."
Related Words
lordotic (adj) kyphosis (posterior thoracic curve) scoliosis (lateral curve) neutral spine alignment anterior pelvic tilt (associated finding)
Word 5 of 5
palpation
/pælˈpeɪ.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
Procedures & Interventions
Medical Definition
A clinical examination technique in which the clinician uses their hands or fingers to feel structures of the body, assessing for tenderness, temperature, texture, swelling, muscle tone, and anatomical abnormalities. One of the four core physical examination methods alongside inspection, percussion, and auscultation.
繁體中文
觸診
Common Collocations
soft tissue palpation tenderness on palpation palpation of the spine palpation reveals deep palpation
OET Clinical Example
"Palpation of the lumbar paraspinal musculature elicited localised tenderness at L3–L4 with palpable muscle guarding, most pronounced on the right side, consistent with the patient's reported pain distribution."
Related Words
palpate (verb) palpable (adj — can be felt) inspection (visual examination) percussion (tapping technique) auscultation (listening technique)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 7)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr. David Huang, a 52-year-old male with a two-year history of relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis, was referred to the outpatient physiotherapy department for a comprehensive gait rehabilitation programme following a recent relapse with residual motor and communication deficits.

On initial assessment, the patient demonstrated significant cerebellar ataxia, presenting with a wide-based gait, positive Romberg test, and marked dysmetria on finger-nose testing bilaterally, which was impeding his ability to perform safe independent transfers. He also presented with expressive aphasia following a concurrent left hemispheric lesion; he demonstrated preserved comprehension but markedly reduced verbal output, requiring augmentative communication strategies during sessions.

Palpation of the lumbar spine revealed no focal bony tenderness; however, postural assessment identified increased lumbar lordosis with associated anterior pelvic tilt, likely contributing to the compensatory gait pattern observed on treadmill analysis. The patient also exhibited mild dysarthria, consistent with prior brainstem involvement documented in his neurology notes, and has an established referral to speech-language therapy.

Goals of physiotherapy include ataxia management through balance and coordination retraining, progressive gait training with appropriate assistive devices, postural correction to address the lumbar lordosis, and coordination with the multidisciplinary team — including speech-language therapy — regarding communication support and fatigue management strategies.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting neurological signs, always specify the assessment tool used (e.g. "Romberg test"), the body region and severity (e.g. "marked dysmetria bilaterally"), and the functional impact (e.g. "impeding safe independent transfers"). This level of precision is essential for OET Writing Band B and above.
