bronchiolitis
/ˌbrɒŋ.ki.əˈlaɪ.tɪs/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
小兒 Paediatric Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
An acute viral infection causing inflammation and obstruction of the small airways (bronchioles); the most common lower respiratory tract infection in infants under two years of age, most frequently caused by respiratory syncytial virus (RSV).
繁體中文
細支氣管炎
Common Collocations
acute bronchiolitis viral bronchiolitis severe bronchiolitis recurrent bronchiolitis bronchiolitis in infancy
OET Clinical Example
"Liam, aged 8 months, was admitted with a 3-day history of worsening respiratory distress consistent with acute bronchiolitis; he required supplemental oxygen and nasogastric feeding due to poor oral intake secondary to increased work of breathing."
Related Words
bronchitis (larger airways) RSV (respiratory syncytial virus) bronchiolitic (adj) wheeze (associated symptom) stridor (upper airway — cf.)
Word 2 of 5
hypoglycaemia
/ˌhaɪ.pəʊ.ɡlaɪˈsiː.mi.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
小兒 Paediatric Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
An abnormally low concentration of glucose in the blood; a common metabolic emergency in neonates and infants that can cause neurological damage if left untreated. Defined as a blood glucose level below 2.6 mmol/L in neonates.
繁體中文
低血糖（症）
Common Collocations
neonatal hypoglycaemia symptomatic hypoglycaemia transient hypoglycaemia rebound hypoglycaemia hypoglycaemia management
OET Clinical Example
"The neonatal team documented an episode of hypoglycaemia (BGL 1.8 mmol/L) at four hours of life, requiring prompt dextrose gel administration and close blood glucose monitoring every 30 minutes until stabilisation."
Related Words
hyperglycaemia (antonym — high blood sugar) hypoglycaemic (adj) blood glucose level / BGL glucagon (emergency treatment) dextrose (treatment substrate)
Word 3 of 5
phototherapy
/ˌfəʊ.təʊˈθer.ə.pi/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
小兒 Paediatric Procedures & Interventions
Medical Definition
The therapeutic use of specific wavelengths of light (typically blue-green light, 460–490 nm) to treat neonatal jaundice; the light converts unconjugated bilirubin in the skin to a water-soluble isomer that can be excreted in urine and bile without hepatic conjugation.
繁體中文
光照療法；光療
Common Collocations
commence phototherapy discontinue phototherapy intensive phototherapy phototherapy threshold under phototherapy
OET Clinical Example
"Phototherapy was commenced at 18 hours of age following serum bilirubin levels exceeding the treatment threshold for a term infant; the parents were counselled regarding the procedure, the importance of adequate hydration, and expected duration of treatment."
Related Words
jaundice (indication) bilirubin (target substance) phototherapeutic (adj) exchange transfusion (more intensive alternative) neonatal jaundice / hyperbilirubinaemia
Word 4 of 5
dehydration
/ˌdiː.haɪˈdreɪ.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
小兒 Paediatric Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A state in which the body loses more fluid than it takes in, resulting in insufficient water to maintain normal physiological functions; classified as mild, moderate, or severe based on the estimated percentage of body weight lost as fluid.
繁體中文
脫水
Common Collocations
mild/moderate/severe dehydration signs of dehydration dehydration in infants clinical dehydration assess for dehydration
OET Clinical Example
"On presentation, the 14-month-old demonstrated clinical signs consistent with moderate dehydration, including dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, and a sunken anterior fontanelle, requiring prompt oral rehydration therapy."
Related Words
rehydration (treatment) dehydrated (adj) fluid deficit hypovolaemia (severe end) oral rehydration solution / ORS
Word 5 of 5
convulsion
/kənˈvʌl.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
小兒 Paediatric Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A sudden, involuntary episode of abnormal and excessive neuronal activity causing violent, uncontrolled muscle contractions and altered consciousness; synonymous with seizure. In children, commonly triggered by fever (febrile convulsion).
繁體中文
抽搐；驚厥
Common Collocations
febrile convulsion tonic-clonic convulsion generalised convulsion recurrent convulsions convulsion management
OET Clinical Example
"Zara, aged 18 months, presented to the emergency department following a 2-minute generalised tonic-clonic convulsion at home associated with a temperature of 39.2°C; she was afebrile on arrival and had returned to her neurological baseline within 20 minutes of the episode."
Related Words
seizure (synonym) epilepsy (chronic seizure disorder) convulsive (adj) anticonvulsant (medication) postictal (post-seizure state)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 46)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

James, a 10-month-old male, was admitted to the paediatric ward from the emergency department following a 4-day history of cough, nasal congestion, and increasing respiratory distress consistent with acute bronchiolitis.

On admission, James demonstrated signs of moderate dehydration, including reduced wet nappies over the preceding 24 hours, dry mucous membranes, and mildly sunken fontanelle. He was commenced on nasogastric rehydration given his inability to maintain adequate oral intake due to the increased work of breathing.

During the admission, the nursing team noted a brief generalised convulsion lasting approximately 90 seconds, associated with a temperature of 38.9°C. The episode was self-terminating. Blood glucose monitoring was performed to exclude concurrent hypoglycaemia (BGL 5.2 mmol/L — within normal limits).

Physiotherapy was consulted regarding airway clearance positioning and parental education on supportive management. Parents were reassured regarding the expected clinical course and provided with clear discharge criteria, including adequate hydration, resolution of respiratory distress, and absence of further convulsive episodes.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting paediatric assessments, always include age in months for infants and young children, specify duration of acute symptoms, and record functional impact (e.g. feeding difficulty, wet nappies). Use objective measures where possible (BGL values, temperature readings, oxygen saturation) to support clinical decisions.
