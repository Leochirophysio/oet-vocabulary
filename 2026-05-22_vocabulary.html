encephalopathy
/ɪnˌsɛf.ə.ˈlɒp.ə.θi/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A general term for any disease or disorder that affects the structure or function of the brain, resulting in altered consciousness, cognition, or behaviour. May be caused by metabolic disturbances, toxins, infection, hypoxia, or trauma.
繁體中文
腦病變；腦功能障礙
Common Collocations
hypoxic encephalopathy hepatic encephalopathy metabolic encephalopathy Wernicke's encephalopathy acute encephalopathy
OET Clinical Example
"Mr. Tui was admitted with features consistent with hypoxic encephalopathy following cardiac arrest, including a Glasgow Coma Scale score of 10, marked disorientation, and impaired short-term memory."
Related Words
encephalopathic (adj) encephalitis (inflammation of the brain) myelopathy (spinal cord disease) neuropathy (peripheral nerve disease) altered consciousness (clinical sign)
Word 2 of 5
flaccidity
/fləˈsɪd.ɪ.ti/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Decreased or absent muscle tone, causing limpness of a muscle or limb. Typically associated with lower motor neurone lesions, peripheral nerve injury, or the acute phase following spinal cord injury or stroke. Contrasted with spasticity, which involves increased tone.
繁體中文
肌肉鬆弛；弛緩性（肌張力低下）
Common Collocations
flaccidity of the limb marked flaccidity post-stroke flaccidity flaccid paralysis flaccid muscle tone
OET Clinical Example
"Initial neurological assessment post-stroke revealed flaccidity of the left upper limb, with absent voluntary movement against gravity and reduced deep tendon reflexes throughout."
Related Words
flaccid (adj) spasticity (antonym — increased tone) hypotonia (low tone, broader term) lower motor neurone lesion (cause) flaccid paralysis
Word 3 of 5
dysmetria
/dɪsˈmiː.tri.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A cerebellar sign characterised by an inability to accurately judge the distance or amplitude of a movement, resulting in overshooting (hypermetria) or undershooting (hypometria) of a target. Classically assessed with the finger-nose test or heel-shin test.
繁體中文
辨距不良；測距障礙（小腦症狀）
Common Collocations
upper limb dysmetria cerebellar dysmetria bilateral dysmetria dysmetria on finger-nose testing truncal dysmetria
OET Clinical Example
"Cerebellar function testing revealed bilateral upper limb dysmetria, with consistent past-pointing on the finger-nose test and an associated intention tremor, indicating cerebellar pathway involvement."
Related Words
ataxia (broader coordination disorder) intention tremor (associated sign) hypermetria (overshooting) hypometria (undershooting) dysdiadochokinesia (impaired alternating movements)
Word 4 of 5
elicit
/ɪˈlɪs.ɪt/ verb 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
Patient Communication
Medical Definition
To draw out or obtain information, a response, or a clinical sign from a patient through skilled questioning, examination, or testing. A core skill in history-taking, patient assessment, and clinical communication.
繁體中文
引出；獲取（病史、反應或體徵）
Common Collocations
elicit a history elicit patient concerns elicit a response elicit symptoms elicit a reflex
OET Clinical Example
"The physiotherapist was able to elicit a comprehensive pain history during the initial consultation, identifying the patient's primary concerns regarding return to work and participation in recreational sport."
Related Words
elicitation (noun) history-taking (clinical process) probe (clinical synonym) draw out (lay equivalent) collateral history (from a third party)

comprehension
/ˌkɒm.prɪˈhɛn.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical

Medical Definition
The ability to understand and process spoken or written information. Clinically, it refers to a patient's capacity to understand instructions, diagnoses, and treatment plans. Also used in neurological assessment to evaluate receptive language function, particularly following stroke or acquired brain injury.
繁體中文
理解力；語言理解能力
Common Collocations
verbal comprehension impaired comprehension reading comprehension patient comprehension comprehension deficits
OET Clinical Example
"Neurological language assessment indicated markedly impaired verbal comprehension, with the patient unable to follow two-step commands reliably, consistent with receptive aphasia secondary to left hemisphere infarction."
Related Words
comprehend (verb) comprehensible (adj) receptive aphasia (neurological — impaired comprehension) health literacy (patient education context) understanding (lay synonym)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 31)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr. James Tui, a 55-year-old male, was admitted to the acute neurology ward following a right-hemisphere ischaemic stroke. He was referred to physiotherapy for inpatient neurological assessment and early rehabilitation.

Initial neurological screening identified features consistent with encephalopathy secondary to post-ictal hypoxia, including marked disorientation, fluctuating consciousness, and a Glasgow Coma Scale score of 12/15. The physiotherapist was able to elicit a limited clinical history via collateral information provided by the patient's wife, as the patient's verbal comprehension was significantly impaired; he was unable to follow simple one-step commands consistently, suggesting concurrent receptive aphasia.

Upper limb assessment revealed flaccidity of the left upper limb, with absent voluntary movement against gravity and reduced deep tendon reflexes throughout. Cerebellar function testing of the right upper limb demonstrated bilateral dysmetria on the finger-nose test, with consistent past-pointing and an associated intention tremor, indicating involvement beyond the primary motor cortex.

Short-term rehabilitation goals include positioning to prevent soft tissue complications associated with upper limb flaccidity, facilitation of early mobilisation as medical stability allows, and ongoing neurological monitoring. Family education was initiated regarding the patient's communication difficulties and strategies to support comprehension during therapy sessions.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting neurological findings in a referral or handover, always specify the side affected (e.g. "left upper limb"), the nature of the tone deficit (flaccidity vs spasticity), and any functional implications (e.g. "unable to follow one-step commands"). Quantify where possible — use the GCS score rather than "confused," and name specific tests performed (e.g. "finger-nose test") to demonstrate clinical rigour.
