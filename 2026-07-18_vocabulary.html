fibrosis
/faɪˈbrəʊ.sɪs/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
The formation of excess fibrous connective tissue (scar tissue) in an organ or tissue as a reparative or reactive process following injury, inflammation, or chronic disease. Pulmonary fibrosis refers specifically to scarring of lung parenchyma, leading to reduced compliance and impaired gas exchange.
繁體中文
纖維化

OET Clinical Example

Related Words

Medical Definition
The central compartment of the thoracic cavity, situated between the two pleural sacs and bounded anteriorly by the sternum and posteriorly by the vertebral column. It contains the heart, great vessels, trachea, oesophagus, thymus, and lymph nodes.

Medical Definition
Relating to or characteristic of pleurisy; most commonly used to describe sharp, stabbing chest pain that is aggravated by deep inspiration, coughing, or movement, arising from inflammation of the parietal pleura.

presyncope
/priːˈsɪŋ.kə.pi/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A sensation of impending loss of consciousness characterised by lightheadedness, dizziness, blurred vision, and weakness, without actual syncope occurring. Often indicates transient cerebral hypoperfusion and warrants clinical investigation prior to exercise rehabilitation.
繁體中文
暈厥前兆；前昏厥狀態
Common Collocations
presyncope on exertion recurrent presyncope episode of presyncope presyncope and palpitations postural presyncope
OET Clinical Example
"The patient reported two episodes of presyncope in the preceding week, each occurring upon rising from a seated position and resolving spontaneously within 30 seconds; exercise testing has therefore been deferred pending cardiology review."
Related Words
syncope (actual loss of consciousness) presyncopal (adj) near-syncope (lay/clinical alternative) orthostatic hypotension (common cause) vasovagal episode (neurocardiogenic cause)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 71)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr David Parata, a 59-year-old former industrial painter with a 30 pack-year smoking history, was referred to the physiotherapy respiratory rehabilitation service by his respiratory physician following a diagnosis of pulmonary fibrosis three months ago.

On initial physiotherapy assessment, Mr Parata reported a significant increase in expectoration of white, mucoid sputum over the past two weeks, producing approximately 20 mL per day with associated productive cough. He also described two recent episodes of presyncope during light exertion, each lasting approximately 20–30 seconds and resolving spontaneously with cessation of activity; these have been reported to his physician and cardiology review is pending.

In addition, he described intermittent, sharp, left-sided pleuritic chest pain aggravated by deep inspiration, consistent with an inflammatory pleural component superimposed on his underlying condition. A recent chest radiograph noted minor mediastinal deviation that was deemed clinically insignificant by the respiratory team.

Physiotherapy goals include airway clearance techniques to facilitate expectoration, graded exercise reconditioning within haemodynamically safe limits, and education regarding activity pacing and symptom monitoring. Commencement of formal exercise testing has been withheld pending cardiology clearance in view of the reported presyncope.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When describing chest symptoms, specify the character (sharp/dull/pleuritic), location (left-sided/right-sided/central), and aggravating factors (inspiration/coughing/movement). Always flag safety concerns (e.g. presyncope, mediastinal changes) and note whether specialist review is pending before proceeding with physiotherapy interventions.
