Word 1 of 5
hypercapnia
/ˌhaɪ.pəˈkæp.ni.ə/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Abnormally elevated levels of carbon dioxide (CO₂) in the arterial blood, typically resulting from inadequate alveolar ventilation; a hallmark of ventilatory failure in conditions such as severe COPD or neuromuscular disease.
繁體中文
高碳酸血症
Common Collocations
acute hypercapnia chronic hypercapnia hypercapnic respiratory failure compensated hypercapnia hypercapnia on ABG
OET Clinical Example
"Arterial blood gas analysis revealed significant hypercapnia (PaCO₂ 68 mmHg) alongside a pH of 7.28, indicating acute-on-chronic ventilatory failure requiring urgent non-invasive ventilatory support."
Related Words
hypocapnia (antonym — low CO₂) hypercapnic (adj) hypoventilation (cause) respiratory acidosis (consequence) CO₂ retention (lay clinical term)
Word 2 of 5
defibrillation
/dɪˌfɪb.rɪˈleɪ.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Procedures & Interventions
Medical Definition
An emergency medical procedure in which a controlled electric shock is delivered to the heart to terminate life-threatening arrhythmias such as ventricular fibrillation or pulseless ventricular tachycardia, with the aim of restoring a normal sinus rhythm.
繁體中文
去顫／電擊除顫
Common Collocations
emergency defibrillation successful defibrillation external defibrillation defibrillation attempt defibrillation threshold
OET Clinical Example
"Following witnessed cardiac arrest with ventricular fibrillation, the patient received two rounds of external defibrillation with successful restoration of sinus rhythm, and was subsequently transferred to the coronary care unit for continuous monitoring."
Related Words
defibrillate (verb) cardioversion (elective form) AED (automated external defibrillator) ICD (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator) fibrillation (arrhythmia treated)
Word 3 of 5
stridor
/ˈstraɪ.dɔː/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A harsh, high-pitched, musical breathing sound produced by turbulent airflow through a partially obstructed upper airway (larynx or trachea); most commonly heard on inspiration and indicative of a potentially serious or life-threatening airway compromise.
繁體中文
喘鳴（上呼吸道阻塞音）
Common Collocations
inspiratory stridor expiratory stridor biphasic stridor audible stridor at rest stridor with distress
OET Clinical Example
"The patient presented with audible inspiratory stridor, intercostal retractions, and marked accessory muscle use, consistent with significant upper airway obstruction; urgent ENT review was arranged."
Related Words
wheeze (lower airway — different location) croup (common paediatric cause) laryngospasm (cause) stridulous (adj) upper airway obstruction (consequence)
Word 4 of 5
ventilation
/ˌven.tɪˈleɪ.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Procedures & Interventions
Medical Definition
The movement of air into and out of the lungs to facilitate gas exchange; clinically used to describe both spontaneous breathing and artificial (mechanical) respiratory support when a patient is unable to maintain adequate ventilation independently.
繁體中文
通氣 / 呼吸支援
Common Collocations
mechanical ventilation non-invasive ventilation (NIV) invasive ventilation positive pressure ventilation ventilation/perfusion (V/Q) mismatch
OET Clinical Example
"The patient was commenced on non-invasive ventilation (NIV) at inspiratory pressure 14 cmH₂O and expiratory pressure 4 cmH₂O in response to worsening hypercapnic respiratory failure, with a marked reduction in work of breathing observed within four hours."
Related Words
ventilate (verb) ventilator (device) NIV / CPAP / BiPAP (modes) perfusion (paired concept) alveolar ventilation (specific measure)
Word 5 of 5
secretions
/sɪˈkriː.ʃənz/ noun (plural) 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Fluids or mucus produced and released by glands or cells; in respiratory physiotherapy, the term refers specifically to airway mucus that accumulates within the bronchial tree and may require active clearance techniques to prevent infection, atelectasis, or respiratory compromise.
繁體中文
分泌物（痰液）
Common Collocations
retained secretions excessive secretions thick/tenacious secretions secretion clearance purulent secretions
OET Clinical Example
"Mr. Nguyen was referred for chest physiotherapy in the context of retained airway secretions, reduced cough effectiveness, and progressive right lower lobe consolidation; airway clearance techniques including the active cycle of breathing were initiated."
Related Words
sputum (expectorated secretions) mucus (lay/anatomical term) exudate (wound secretions) mucolytic (agent that thins secretions) airway clearance (removal technique)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 34)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr. David Park, a 72-year-old male with a background of severe COPD (GOLD Stage IV) and type 2 respiratory failure, was admitted to the respiratory ward following a three-day history of worsening breathlessness and productive cough.

On admission, arterial blood gas analysis confirmed acute-on-chronic hypercapnia (PaCO₂ 74 mmHg, pH 7.24), and he was commenced on bilevel non-invasive ventilation at settings of IPAP 18 cmH₂O / EPAP 5 cmH₂O, with significant symptomatic improvement within six hours. He was haemodynamically stable throughout; there was no requirement for defibrillation or other cardiac intervention.

Respiratory assessment revealed diffuse expiratory wheeze and widespread coarse crepitations bilaterally, with substantial secretions noted on suction. Cough was weak and ineffective. There was no evidence of stridor or upper airway compromise.

Mr. Park is referred to the physiotherapy team for airway clearance, optimisation of secretion management, and weaning support as non-invasive ventilation is gradually de-escalated. Breathing retraining and a supported early mobilisation programme are also requested once his clinical condition stabilises.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting respiratory interventions, always specify the mode and settings of ventilatory support (e.g. "IPAP 18 / EPAP 5"), the character of secretions (e.g. "purulent / tenacious"), and the patient response (e.g. "symptomatic improvement within six hours"). Precise clinical language distinguishes a strong OET response from a vague one.
