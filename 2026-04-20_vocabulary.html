crepitus
/ˈkrɛp.ɪ.təs/ noun 🔊 Cambridge
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A grating, crackling, or popping sensation or sound produced by movement of a joint or tendon, caused by rough surfaces moving against each other.
繁體中文
捻髮音；關節摩擦音
Common Collocations
audible crepitus palpable crepitus crepitus on movement crepitus at the knee fine crepitus
OET Clinical Example
"On assessment, palpable crepitus was noted at the patellofemoral joint bilaterally, consistent with the reported diagnosis of osteoarthritis."
Related Words
crepitation (variant) clicking (lay term) grinding (lay term) crepitant (adj)

mobilisation
/ˌməʊ.bɪ.laɪˈzeɪ.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge
骨科 Musculoskeletal Procedures & Interventions
Medical Definition
A manual therapy technique in which a clinician applies controlled, graded passive movements to a joint or soft tissue to restore range of motion and reduce pain.
繁體中文
關節鬆動術；活動化治療
Common Collocations
joint mobilisation spinal mobilisation graded mobilisation early mobilisation mobilisation techniques
OET Clinical Example
"A course of lumbar mobilisation was commenced, alongside core stabilisation exercises, with the aim of improving range of motion and reducing the patient's reported pain levels."
Related Words
manipulation (higher velocity) mobilise (verb) immobilisation (antonym) manual therapy

Word 5 of 5
contracture
/kənˈtræk.tʃər/ noun 🔊 Cambridge
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A permanent shortening and hardening of a muscle, tendon, or joint capsule that results in reduced range of motion and joint deformity, often due to fibrosis, spasticity, or prolonged immobilisation.
繁體中文
攣縮；肌肉/關節攣縮
Common Collocations
flexion contracture hip contracture prevent contracture contracture management soft tissue contracture
OET Clinical Example
"A fixed flexion contracture of 20° was noted at the right hip, significantly limiting the patient's walking tolerance and functional independence."
Related Words
spasticity (neurological cause) fibrosis (tissue cause) stretching (treatment) splinting (prevention) range of motion (ROM)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 2)

Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context.

Mr. James Tanner, a 72-year-old male with a background of right-sided hemiplegia following a cerebrovascular accident eight months prior, was referred to the outpatient physiotherapy department for ongoing rehabilitation.

On initial assessment, inferior glenohumeral subluxation of the right shoulder was confirmed on palpation, with the patient reporting intermittent aching pain at rest. A fixed elbow flexion contracture of approximately 15° was also noted, limiting his ability to perform overhead activities and personal care tasks independently.

Assessment of the right lower limb revealed palpable crepitus at the knee joint on passive range of motion testing. Proprioception was markedly impaired in the right ankle, contributing to an unsafe and asymmetrical gait pattern.

The physiotherapy programme will include mobilisation of the right shoulder and elbow to address soft tissue tightness, proprioceptive retraining, and progressive gait rehabilitation.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When describing multiple findings, organise by body region (shoulder → elbow → knee → ankle) for a logical, easy-to-follow structure. Use objective measurements where possible (e.g. "15° flexion contracture").
