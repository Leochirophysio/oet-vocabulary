intercostal
/ˌɪn.təˈkɒs.təl/ adjective 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
心肺 Cardiopulmonary Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Situated or occurring between adjacent ribs; relating to the muscles, nerves, vessels, or spaces that lie between the ribs, which are clinically important in respiratory assessment and chest drain insertion.
繁體中文
肋間的
Common Collocations
intercostal muscles intercostal recession intercostal drain intercostal neuralgia intercostal space
OET Clinical Example
"On respiratory assessment, marked intercostal recession was observed bilaterally along with accessory muscle recruitment, indicating increased work of breathing; the patient was repositioned in high side-lying to optimise ventilation and supplemental oxygen was commenced."
Related Words
costal (relating to ribs) subcostal (below the ribs) suprasternal (above the sternum) recession (inward movement during breathing) thoracic (relating to the chest)

Medical Definition
A complex metabolic syndrome associated with underlying chronic illness (e.g. cancer, COPD, heart failure), characterised by profound involuntary weight loss, skeletal muscle wasting, fatigue, weakness, and anorexia that cannot be fully reversed by nutritional support alone.

Related Words

✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 29)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr William Turner, a 74-year-old retired coal miner, was admitted to the respiratory ward following an acute exacerbation of his known emphysema. On assessment, he presented with severe breathlessness at rest and marked intercostal recession with active recruitment of accessory muscles, consistent with significantly increased work of breathing.

Concurrent investigations confirmed a right-sided pleural effusion, for which an intercostal drain was inserted at the fifth intercostal space, mid-axillary line, with immediate improvement in ventilatory comfort.

Of note, Mr Turner also demonstrates features of cachexia secondary to his chronic respiratory disease, with a BMI of 17.1 kg/m² and significant bilateral upper and lower limb muscle wasting. He additionally developed dysphagia to thin fluids during this admission, attributed to generalised deconditioning and fatigue; a modified diet of IDDSI Level 3 liquidised foods has been introduced pending formal speech-language therapy review.

Physiotherapy input is requested to address secretion clearance, positioning for optimised ventilation, and a graded mobility programme once the patient is medically stable.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When writing referral or handover notes for patients with chronic respiratory disease, always document current work of breathing (e.g. "intercostal recession"), nutritional status (BMI, cachexia), and any swallowing concerns (dysphagia grade, current diet level). Precise, specific language demonstrates clinical competence in the OET Writing sub-test.
