fasciculation
/fəˌsɪk.jʊˈleɪ.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A brief, spontaneous, involuntary contraction of a small group of muscle fibres (a motor unit), visible as a localised twitching under the skin; often associated with lower motor neurone lesions or nerve irritation.
繁體中文
肌束顫動
Common Collocations
muscle fasciculations benign fasciculation syndrome tongue fasciculations fasciculations at rest visible fasciculations
OET Clinical Example
"On neurological examination, fine fasciculations were observed in the bilateral intrinsic hand muscles; the patient has been referred urgently to neurology to exclude a lower motor neurone lesion."
Related Words
fasciculate (verb) fascicular (adj) fibrillation (finer — single fibre) tremor (rhythmic shaking) clonus (rhythmic, reflex-driven)

enthesopathy
/ˌen.θɪˈsɒ.pə.θi/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Procedures & Interventions
Medical Definition
A disease or disorder at the enthesis — the site where a tendon, ligament, or joint capsule attaches to bone; characterised by pain, degeneration, or calcification at the attachment point.
繁體中文
附著點病變；肌腱附著點病
Common Collocations
insertional enthesopathy Achilles enthesopathy patellar enthesopathy enthesopathic change reactive enthesopathy
OET Clinical Example
"Ultrasound confirmed insertional enthesopathy at the calcaneal attachment of the Achilles tendon; the patient has been referred for physiotherapy to address load management, activity modification, and a graduated eccentric strengthening programme."
Related Words
enthesis (noun — attachment site) enthesitis (inflammatory type) entheseal (adj) tendinopathy (broader tendon disorder) calcific tendinitis (calcification variant)

nociception
/ˌnəʊ.sɪˈsep.ʃən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
神經 Neurological Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
The neural process by which the nervous system detects, encodes, and transmits signals from potentially tissue-damaging (noxious) stimuli; the sensory basis of pain perception, distinct from the subjective experience of pain itself.
繁體中文
傷害性感受；痛覺感受
Common Collocations
nociceptive pain peripheral nociception central nociception nociceptive threshold sensitised nociception
OET Clinical Example
"The patient's ongoing pain presentation is consistent with sensitised nociception rather than ongoing tissue damage, and a biopsychosocial approach incorporating graded exposure has been incorporated into the rehabilitation plan."
Related Words
nociceptor (noun — receptor) nociceptive (adj) noxious stimulus (trigger) central sensitisation (amplified processing) allodynia (pain from non-painful stimuli)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 14)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr. Tama Herewini, a 34-year-old male musician, was referred to your physiotherapy service by his general practitioner with a six-month history of right forearm pain and involuntary muscle contractions affecting his performance.

Neurological examination revealed intermittent fasciculations in the right flexor digitorum superficialis, visible at rest, which have been noted by the referring clinician and are currently under investigation by neurology. In addition, the patient has been assessed for task-specific dystonia, a known occupational hazard in professional musicians, which is contributing to abnormal finger posturing during sustained playing.

Musculoskeletal assessment identified significant laxity of the right medial elbow collateral ligament complex, likely secondary to repetitive valgus loading, alongside imaging-confirmed enthesopathy at the common flexor origin. Pain assessment indicated that the patient's symptoms are partially driven by sensitised nociception secondary to central sensitisation, and a pain neuroscience education component has been incorporated into the physiotherapy programme.

Goals of intervention include pain neuroscience education, graded load management for the enthesopathy, joint stabilisation strategies to address ligamentous laxity, and coordination with the neurology team regarding the dystonia and fasciculation findings.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When writing referral letters involving multiple diagnoses, address each finding systematically — neurological findings first, then musculoskeletal, then functional impact. Use precise anatomical language (e.g. "common flexor origin", "medial elbow collateral ligament complex") rather than vague descriptors like "elbow area."
