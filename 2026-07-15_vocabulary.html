sprain
/spreɪn/ noun, verb 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
An injury to a ligament caused by excessive stretching or tearing beyond its normal range; classified by severity — Grade I (microscopic tearing with intact stability), Grade II (partial tear with some instability), Grade III (complete rupture with gross instability).
繁體中文
扭傷；韌帶拉傷
Common Collocations
lateral ankle sprain Grade II sprain ligamentous sprain sprain with associated instability recurrent sprain
OET Clinical Example
"The patient sustained a Grade II lateral ankle sprain during recreational football six weeks prior, presenting with residual swelling, pain on weight-bearing, and subjective instability; physiotherapy is requested to address functional rehabilitation and prevent recurrence."
Related Words
strain (muscle/tendon injury) ligamentous tear (severe form) sprained (adj) Grade I / II / III (severity scale) instability (sequela)

periarticular
/ˌper.i.ɑːˈtɪk.jʊ.lə/ adjective 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
Situated or occurring in the tissues immediately surrounding a joint, including the joint capsule, adjacent ligaments, tendons, and bursae; used to describe pathological changes in structures that are close to but outside the joint cavity itself.
繁體中文
關節周圍的
Common Collocations

OET Clinical Example

Related Words
intra-articular (within the joint) extra-articular (outside the joint) capsular (relating to the joint capsule) periarticular ossification (bone formation near joint) juxta-articular (immediately adjacent)

exostosis
/ˌek.sɒˈstəʊ.sɪs/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical
骨科 Musculoskeletal Signs & Symptoms
Medical Definition
A benign bony outgrowth projecting from the surface of a bone, commonly known as a bone spur; may develop at tendon or ligament insertion sites in response to chronic mechanical stress, and can impinge on adjacent soft tissues causing localised pain and functional limitation.
繁體中文
骨贅；外生骨疣
Common Collocations
calcaneal exostosis posterior exostosis bony exostosis exostosis causing impingement subungual exostosis
OET Clinical Example
"Plain radiograph of the right heel confirmed a prominent posterior calcaneal exostosis at the Achilles insertion, which correlates with the patient's insertional Achilles pain and reduced ankle dorsiflexion noted on clinical assessment."
Related Words
osteophyte (joint surface spur) bone spur (lay term) exostoses (plural) osteochondroma (cartilage-capped variant) retrocalcaneal impingement (clinical consequence)

Medical Definition
Reduced or diminished sensitivity to sensory stimulation, particularly to light touch or temperature, in a specific area of the body; typically indicates peripheral nerve damage, nerve root compression, or central nervous system pathology.
繁體中文
感覺減退
Common Collocations

OET Clinical Example

Related Words
hyperaesthesia (increased sensitivity — antonym) anaesthesia (complete loss of sensation) paraesthesia (abnormal sensation) allodynia (pain from non-painful stimulus) hypoesthesia (American spelling)

/ədˈhiː.ʒən/ noun 🔊 Cambridge 📖 M-W Medical

Medical Definition
An abnormal band of fibrous scar tissue that binds together structures which are normally separate or freely mobile; develops following surgery, injury, or prolonged inflammation, and commonly restricts joint range of motion or causes pain in musculoskeletal conditions such as adhesive capsulitis.
繁體中文
沾黏；組織沾連

OET Clinical Example

Related Words
adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder) fibrosis (scar tissue formation) capsular contracture (joint-specific) adherent (adjective) lysis of adhesions (surgical release)
✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 68)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr Daniel Park, a 34-year-old recreational touch rugby player, is referred to your physiotherapy department following a right lateral ankle sprain sustained three weeks ago during a match. He was initially managed in the emergency department, where plain radiographs excluded fracture and the injury was graded as Grade II based on the presence of partial anterior talofibular ligament disruption on ultrasound.

On assessment today, he presents with residual periarticular swelling over the lateral malleolus, warmth, and point tenderness on palpation of the anterior talofibular and calcaneofibular ligaments. Active range of motion is reduced, with dorsiflexion limited to 8 degrees due to pain and guarding. Neurological screening revealed mild hypoaesthesia to light touch over the dorsum of the foot in the superficial peroneal nerve distribution, likely attributable to localised oedema and haematoma pressure rather than nerve injury.

Incidentally, plain X-ray obtained at presentation also noted a small posterior calcaneal exostosis, which may have contributed to his pre-existing insertional discomfort and should be addressed in the medium-term rehabilitation plan.

Goals of physiotherapy include progressive weight-bearing rehabilitation, proprioceptive retraining, peroneal strengthening, and patient education regarding ankle taping to minimise the risk of recurrent sprain. Return to sport is anticipated at six to eight weeks provided functional milestones are achieved.

📌 OET Writing Tip: In musculoskeletal referral letters, always specify injury grade (e.g. "Grade II sprain"), the specific structure involved (e.g. "anterior talofibular ligament"), and neurological screening findings — even if normal. Use anatomical precision rather than lay terms such as "rolled ankle."
