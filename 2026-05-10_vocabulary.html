Medical Definition

✍️ OET Writing Task — Clinical Scenario (Day 21)

Read the scenario below. Notice how today's vocabulary is used in a realistic OET-style referral context. Highlighted words are from today's list.

Mr. Chen Wei, a 71-year-old retired engineer with a background of idiopathic Parkinson's disease (diagnosed 2019) and bilateral knee osteoarthritis, was referred to your physiotherapy outpatient department for multidisciplinary rehabilitation.

On neurological assessment, he demonstrated significant bradykinesia affecting bilateral upper limb dexterity and gait initiation, with a characteristic shuffling gait pattern and reduced stride length. A bilateral resting tremor was noted in both hands (right greater than left), rated 2/4 on the Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale. Following a minor fall three months ago, he also presented with mild left-sided hemiparesis (MRC grade 4/5 throughout) attributed to a small lacunar infarct identified on CT brain, now under neurology follow-up.

Musculoskeletal review of the right knee revealed crepitus on patellofemoral compression, consistent with known chondromalacia patellae (Grade II, confirmed on MRI January 2026). X-ray of the lumbar spine demonstrated multilevel osteophyte formation at L3/4 and L4/5, with mild foraminal narrowing bilaterally, contributing to his reported lower back stiffness.

Physiotherapy goals include gait retraining with cueing strategies, fall prevention education, progressive lower limb strengthening to address patellofemoral loading, and postural exercise to manage lumbar degeneration symptoms.

📌 OET Writing Tip: When documenting neurological signs, specify laterality (left/right/bilateral), severity grading (e.g. MRC 4/5, UPDRS 2/4), and functional impact (e.g. "affecting gait initiation"). For musculoskeletal findings, reference imaging confirmation and grade where applicable. Avoid vague descriptors like "some weakness" or "a bit slow."
